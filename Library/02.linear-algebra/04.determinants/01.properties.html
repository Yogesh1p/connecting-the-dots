A Single Number That Says Everything

We now make a significant turn. Up to this point, much of our work has involved rectangular matrices: projections, least squares, column spaces. From here on, we focus on square matrices, and the first major topic is the determinant.

The determinant is a single number associated with any square matrix $A$. We write it as $\det A$ or $|A|$. What makes this number so remarkable is how much it encodes. It can tell you, immediately and definitively, whether a matrix is invertible. It connects to the pivots from elimination, to volumes in geometry, and it will soon be the gateway to eigenvalues.

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, you already know the formula:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = ad - bc$$

Rather than immediately trying to extend this formula to larger matrices (which gets complicated fast), we will take a more powerful approach: we define the determinant through a short list of fundamental properties. Everything else follows from these properties alone.

The Three Defining Properties

These three rules completely determine the determinant of any square matrix. Rules 4 through 10 are all consequences of these three.

Property 1: The Identity Has Determinant 1

The most natural starting point: $\det I = 1$. The identity matrix represents the simplest possible transformation—doing nothing—and its determinant reflects that with the simplest possible value.

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = 1$$

Geometrically, think of the identity as a unit square. Its "area" is 1. The determinant, as we will see, is deeply connected to volume.

Property 2: Row Exchanges Reverse the Sign

When you exchange two rows of a matrix, the determinant flips sign: from positive to negative, or from negative to positive.

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} c & d \\ a & b \end{bmatrix} = -\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$$

This rule is why permutation matrices have determinant $+1$ or $-1$. Starting from the identity (determinant $+1$), each row exchange flips the sign. An even number of swaps gives $+1$; an odd number gives $-1$.

Property 3: Linearity in Each Row

The determinant is a linear function of each row, taken separately (with all other rows held fixed). This has two parts:

  1. Scaling: Multiplying one row by $t$ multiplies the determinant by $t$.
    $$\det \begin{bmatrix} ta & tb \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = t \cdot \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$$
  2. Additivity: If a row is a sum, the determinant splits into a sum.
    $$\det \begin{bmatrix} a + a' & b + b' \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} + \det \begin{bmatrix} a' & b' \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$$

A critical warning: linearity does not mean $\det(A + B) = \det A + \det B$. That is false. It means the determinant is linear in one row at a time, holding the rest fixed. For example, $\det 2I = 2^n$, not $2 \cdot \det I$—because scaling every row multiplies the determinant $n$ times over.

Seven Consequences

From those three rules, we can derive everything else we need to know.

Property 4: Equal Rows Give Zero

If two rows of $A$ are identical, then $\det A = 0$. Here is a concrete example:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = (3)(1) - (1)(3) = 0$$

The proof works by contradiction using Property 2. Start with a matrix that has two identical rows, and call its determinant $D$. Now swap those two rows. Because they are identical, the matrix does not change at all — so the determinant must still be $D$. But Property 2 says a row exchange reverses the sign, giving us $-D$. We need $D = -D$, which forces $D = 0$.

We can write this out explicitly for a $2 \times 2$ case. Let the two rows both equal $(a, b)$:

$$\underbrace{\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ a & b \end{bmatrix}}_{= D} \xrightarrow{\text{swap rows}} \underbrace{\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ a & b \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{matrix unchanged}} = \underbrace{-D}_{\text{sign flipped by Property 2}} \implies D = 0$$

Both rows are identical: (3, 1). Call the determinant D.

A matrix with two identical rows is singular — its rows are linearly dependent, so it cannot be inverted. Property 4 is the determinant confirming that fact.

Property 5: Elimination Doesn't Change the Determinant

Subtracting a multiple of one row from another row leaves the determinant unchanged. This is what makes Gaussian elimination so useful for computing determinants.

Let's see it with numbers. Start with:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \det A = (2)(5) - (3)(4) = -2$$

Subtract $2$ times row 1 from row 2 — a standard elimination step:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 - 2(2) & 5 - 2(3) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \det = (2)(-1) - (3)(0) = -2 \checkmark$$

The determinant is unchanged. To see why this always works, apply Property 3 to split the modified row into two separate matrices:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c - \ell a & d - \ell b \end{bmatrix} = \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} + \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ -\ell a & -\ell b \end{bmatrix}$$

The second matrix has its second row equal to $-\ell$ times its first row — they are proportional, which means the two rows are effectively identical (up to a scalar). By Property 4, that determinant is zero. We are left with exactly the original determinant.

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ -\ell a & -\ell b \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{factor } -\ell \text{ from row 2 (Property 3)}} -\ell \cdot \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ a & b \end{bmatrix} = -\ell \cdot 0 = 0$$
Elimination · det unchanged Step 1 of 4
Start: A = [[2,3],[4,5]]. det A = (2)(5)−(3)(4) = −2.

This is a hugely practical result: we can run Gaussian elimination freely without disturbing the determinant — the only exception being row exchanges, which flip the sign by Property 2.

Property 6: A Row of Zeros Gives Zero

If any row of $A$ is all zeros, then $\det A = 0$. For example:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 7 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = (5)(0) - (7)(0) = 0$$

And in $3 \times 3$, it doesn't matter which row is zero:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 2 & 7 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = 0$$

The proof follows directly from Property 3. A zero row is just the zero vector — scaling any row by $t = 0$ turns it into a zero row and multiplies the determinant by $0$:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ t \cdot 0 & t \cdot 0 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{Property 3, factor } t \text{ from row 2}} t \cdot \det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

For this to hold for every $t$, the determinant must be zero. A matrix with a zero row is clearly singular — the rows cannot span the whole space — and Property 6 reflects exactly that.

Property 7: Triangular Matrices — Product of Diagonal

For any triangular matrix, the determinant is the product of the diagonal entries alone. The off-diagonal entries do not matter:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 7 & 2 \\ 0 & 5 & 8 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 4 = 60$$

Here is why. Use Property 5 (elimination) to zero out every entry above the diagonal — this does not change the determinant. After elimination, we have a diagonal matrix:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 7 & 2 \\ 0 & 5 & 8 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{eliminate above diagonal (Property 5)}} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

Now factor each diagonal entry out of its row using Property 3:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 4 \cdot \det \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = 60 \cdot 1 = 60$$

The last step uses Property 1: $\det I = 1$. If any diagonal entry is zero, then that pivot is missing, elimination produces a zero row, and Property 6 gives $\det = 0$. So the formula $d_1 \cdot d_2 \cdots d_n$ handles both cases.

Property 8: Determinant as the Invertibility Test

This is the most important application of properties 1–7. A square matrix $A$ is invertible if and only if $\det A \neq 0$.

The Invertibility Test
$$A \text{ is invertible} \iff \det A \neq 0$$

Let's see both cases with a $2 \times 2$ matrix. First, an invertible matrix:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \frac{3}{2}R_1} U = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & \frac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \det A = 2 \cdot \tfrac{5}{2} = 5 \neq 0 \quad \checkmark \text{ invertible}$$

Now a singular matrix — one where the rows are proportional:

$$B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \frac{1}{2}R_1} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \det B = 2 \cdot 0 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \text{ singular}$$

The connection to elimination is clean. If $A$ is invertible, elimination produces $n$ nonzero pivots $d_1, \ldots, d_n$. By Property 5, elimination didn't change $\det A$, so:

$$\det A = \det U = d_1 \cdot d_2 \cdots d_n \neq 0$$

If $A$ is singular, elimination produces at least one zero row. By Property 6, $\det U = 0$, and therefore $\det A = 0$.

Property 9: The Product Rule

The determinant of a product is the product of the determinants:

$$\det AB = (\det A)(\det B)$$

Let's verify with a concrete example. Take:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \det A = 3 \qquad B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \det B = (4)(2)-(1)(1) = 7$$
$$AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \det(AB) = (6)(6)-(5)(3) = 21 = 3 \cdot 7 \checkmark$$

This is a striking fact. Note that $\det(A + B) \neq \det A + \det B$ in general — but the product rule holds perfectly. Some immediate consequences fall right out:

$$A \cdot A^{-1} = I \implies (\det A)(\det A^{-1}) = 1 \implies \det A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A}$$
$$\det(2A) = 2^n \det A \quad \text{(not } 2\det A\text{)}$$

That last point comes from Property 3: scaling every one of the $n$ rows by $2$ multiplies the determinant by $2$ each time, giving $2^n$ overall. For a $2 \times 2$ matrix with $\det A = 3$:

$$\det(2A) = 2^2 \cdot 3 = 12, \qquad \text{not } 2 \cdot 3 = 6$$

Property 10: Transpose Preserves the Determinant

The transpose of a matrix has the same determinant:

$$\det A^T = \det A$$

We can verify this directly for a $2 \times 2$ matrix. The transpose swaps the off-diagonal entries, but the formula $ad - bc$ is symmetric in those positions:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \det A = (1)(6)-(2)(5) = -4$$
$$A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 5 \\ 2 & 6 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \det A^T = (1)(6)-(5)(2) = -4 \checkmark$$

For larger matrices, the proof runs through $LU$ factorization. Write $A = LU$ (assuming no row exchanges for simplicity). Transposing gives $A^T = U^T L^T$. Now apply Property 9 (product rule) to each side:

$$\det A = \det L \cdot \det U$$ $$\det A^T = \det U^T \cdot \det L^T$$

Both $L$ and $L^T$ are triangular with 1s on the diagonal, so by Property 7: $\det L = \det L^T = 1$. And $U$ and $U^T$ share the same diagonal entries, so by Property 7 again: $\det U = \det U^T$. Therefore $\det A = \det A^T$.

The consequence is immediate and powerful: every rule that applies to rows applies equally to columns. Here are three column facts that follow directly:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \qquad \text{(zero column } \Rightarrow \text{ zero determinant)}$$
$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 7 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = 8, \qquad \det \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 5 & 7 \end{bmatrix} = -8 \qquad \text{(swapping two columns flips sign)}$$
$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3t \\ 4 & 2t \end{bmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{t} \qquad \text{(determinant is linear in each column)}$$

Three Ways to Compute the Determinant

With the ten properties in hand, we now have three distinct strategies for computing $\det A$. They are not competing methods—they are three different windows into the same number. To make the comparison concrete, we will run all three on the same matrix:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

We chose this matrix because it is already upper triangular in the first column, which makes the elimination steps clean and lets us focus on the ideas rather than arithmetic. The answer we are aiming for is $\det A = 36$.

Method 1: The Pivot Formula

This is how computers find determinants of large matrices. The strategy is to run Gaussian elimination on $A$ and read off the pivots from the diagonal of the resulting upper triangular matrix $U$. The determinant is then the product of those pivots, multiplied by $+1$ or $-1$ depending on whether an even or odd number of row exchanges were needed.

Why does this work? Property 5 tells us that subtracting a multiple of one row from another does not change the determinant. So elimination does not change the value of $\det A$—it only reshapes the matrix into something triangular. Then Property 7 gives us the determinant of a triangular matrix for free: it's just the product of the diagonal entries.

Let's eliminate. The matrix already has zeros below the first pivot, so we go straight to eliminating the $(3,2)$ entry. Subtract $\frac{1}{2}$ of row 2 from row 3:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - \frac{1}{2}R_2} U = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac{9}{2} \end{bmatrix}$$

No row exchanges were needed, so the sign is $+1$. The three pivots sitting on the diagonal of $U$ are $2$, $4$, and $\frac{9}{2}$. Their product is the determinant:

$$\det A = (+1) \times 2 \times 4 \times \frac{9}{2} = 36$$

Pivot Formula
$$\det A = (\pm 1) \times d_1 \times d_2 \times \cdots \times d_n$$

where $d_1, \ldots, d_n$ are the pivots of $U$ and the sign tracks row exchanges.

Method 2: The Big Formula

The big formula is the complete, explicit expression for the determinant as a sum over all permutations. It trades computational efficiency for total transparency — every single term is visible and accounted for. To understand where it comes from, we need to watch Property 3 (linearity in each row) do its work.

Step 1: Expand row 1 using linearity

Every row of $A$ can be written as a sum of contributions from individual columns. Property 3 says we can split the determinant on any one row. Applied to row 1 of our matrix:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{2} & \mathbf{1} & \mathbf{3} \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 1 is $[2, 1, 3]$. We split this into its three individual column contributions — each a matrix that keeps only one nonzero entry in row 1:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} + \det \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} + \det \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

We now have three determinants to handle. Apply the same split to row 2 in each of them — row 2 is $[0, 4, 1]$, so it contributes entries in columns 1, 2, and 3:

Step 2: Expand row 2 in each piece — the rule that kills repeats

Focus on the first piece, where row 1 already occupies column 1. When we try to split row 2, we get three sub-terms. But any sub-term that also puts row 2's entry in column 1 creates a matrix with two nonzero entries in column 1 — which means two rows are proportional and the determinant is zero by Property 4. So column 1 is already claimed by row 1; row 2 must choose from columns 2 or 3. The same logic applies to all three pieces:

$$\det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \;\longrightarrow\; \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} + \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

After applying this expansion to all three pieces from Step 1, we end up with $3 \times 2 = 6$ matrices, each with exactly one nonzero entry per row, from a different column. Row 3 then has no choice — only one column remains unclaimed. This is why the total is exactly $3! = 6$ terms.

↗ From Counting: Why $n!$?

The count $3! = 6$ is not a coincidence — it is the number of ways to arrange 3 distinct items in order, which is exactly a permutation. Row 1 has 3 column choices, row 2 has 2 remaining, row 3 has 1. By the multiplication rule: $3 \times 2 \times 1 = 3! = 6$.

For an $n \times n$ matrix the same logic gives $n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 1 = n!$ terms. For $n = 10$ that is already $3{,}628{,}800$ — which is why the big formula is never used for computation.

Permutations (Sampling Without Replacement, Order Matters)

Imagine choosing which column each row of the matrix "claims." Row 1 picks first: it has $n$ options. Row 2 picks next, but one column is already claimed, so it has $n - 1$ options. Row 3 has $n - 2$, and so on. By the Multiplication Rule — if experiment A has $a$ outcomes and experiment B has $b$ outcomes, the compound experiment has $ab$ outcomes — the total number of complete column assignments is:

$$n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \cdots \times 1 = n!$$

This is a permutation of $n$ items taken $n$ at a time — written ${}^nP_n$ — where order matters and no column can be reused. Using the general permutation formula ${}^nP_k = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}$ with $k = n$ gives $\frac{n!}{0!} = n!$.

For $n = 3$: $\;3! = 6$. For $n = 4$: $\;4! = 24$. For $n = 10$: $\;10! = 3{,}628{,}800$.

Elimination is essentially doing this same accounting, but skipping all the zero terms automatically.

How the sign works: counting column swaps

Each permutation $\sigma = (\sigma(1), \sigma(2), \sigma(3))$ tells you which column each row contributes. The sign is $+1$ if the sequence $(\sigma(1), \sigma(2), \sigma(3))$ can be sorted back to $(1,2,3)$ with an even number of adjacent swaps, and $-1$ if it takes an odd number. For example:

$$\sigma = (1, 3, 2): \quad (1, \mathbf{3}, \mathbf{2}) \xrightarrow{\text{swap positions 2,3}} (1, 2, 3) \quad \Rightarrow 1 \text{ swap} \Rightarrow \text{sign} = -1$$
$$\sigma = (2, 3, 1): \quad (\mathbf{2}, \mathbf{3}, 1) \xrightarrow{\text{swap}} (1, 3, \mathbf{2}) \cdot\text{wait} \implies (2,3,1) \xrightarrow{\text{swap 1,2}} (3,2,1) \xrightarrow{\text{swap 2,3}} (3,1,2) \xrightarrow{\text{swap 1,2}} (1,3,2) \xrightarrow{\text{swap 2,3}} (1,2,3)$$

Wait — a cleaner way to count: for $\sigma = (2,3,1)$, the number of inversions (pairs $(i,j)$ where $i < j$ but $\sigma(i) > \sigma(j)$) is 2: the pair $(2,1)$ and $(3,1)$. Two inversions is even, so the sign is $+1$. You can always count inversions as a shortcut instead of simulating swaps.

The Big Formula
$$\det A = \sum_{\sigma} (\operatorname{sign} \sigma)\, a_{1\sigma(1)}\, a_{2\sigma(2)} \cdots a_{n\sigma(n)}$$

Each term picks exactly one entry from each row, never repeating a column. The sign is $+1$ for even permutations, $-1$ for odd. There are $n!$ terms total.

Method 3: The Cofactor Formula

The cofactor formula is recursive. Instead of summing over all $n!$ permutations at once, we break the $n \times n$ determinant into $n$ smaller determinants of size $(n-1) \times (n-1)$. Those smaller determinants can be broken down further, all the way to $2 \times 2$ matrices where we know the answer. This is called cofactor expansion, and it works along any row or any column.

The cofactor $C_{ij}$ of entry $a_{ij}$ is the determinant of the matrix left after deleting row $i$ and column $j$, multiplied by a sign:

$$C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} \cdot M_{ij}$$

where $M_{ij}$ is called the minor—the determinant of the $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ submatrix formed by deleting row $i$ and column $j$. The sign $(-1)^{i+j}$ follows a checkerboard pattern: $+$ when $i+j$ is even, $-$ when $i+j$ is odd.

$$\text{Sign pattern: } \begin{bmatrix} + & - & + \\ - & + & - \\ + & - & + \end{bmatrix}$$

Expanding along row 1 of $A$, we get:

$$\det A = a_{11} C_{11} + a_{12} C_{12} + a_{13} C_{13}$$

Let's compute each cofactor. For $C_{11}$, delete row 1 and column 1, leaving a $2 \times 2$ matrix:

$$C_{11} = (+1) \det \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = (4)(5) - (1)(2) = 18$$

For $C_{12}$, delete row 1 and column 2. The sign is $(-1)^{1+2} = -1$:

$$C_{12} = (-1) \det \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = (-1)\bigl[(0)(5) - (1)(0)\bigr] = 0$$

For $C_{13}$, delete row 1 and column 3. The sign is $(-1)^{1+3} = +1$:

$$C_{13} = (+1) \det \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 4 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = (0)(2) - (4)(0) = 0$$

Now assemble the full expansion:

$$\det A = a_{11} C_{11} + a_{12} C_{12} + a_{13} C_{13} = (2)(18) + (1)(0) + (3)(0) = 36 \checkmark$$

We got lucky here: the zeros in column 1 (below the first entry) made two of the three cofactors vanish before we even computed them. In general, it is always worth expanding along the row or column that has the most zeros—each zero kills one cofactor and saves you a whole sub-determinant calculation.

Cofactor Expansion Along Row $i$
$$\det A = a_{i1} C_{i1} + a_{i2} C_{i2} + \cdots + a_{in} C_{in}$$

where $C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} M_{ij}$ and $M_{ij}$ is the determinant of the submatrix with row $i$ and column $j$ removed.

Which Method to Use?

All three methods give the same answer—they are computing the same number via different paths. The right choice depends on context:

  • Pivot formula — the workhorse. Use it whenever you want a numerical answer, especially for matrices larger than $3 \times 3$. It is the method computers use.
  • Big formula — the theoretical foundation. It proves the determinant is well-defined, connects it to permutations, and gives the $2 \times 2$ formula $ad - bc$ as a special case. Rarely used for direct computation.
  • Cofactor formula — the recursive tool. Most useful for symbolic matrices, deriving formulas for the inverse ($A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} C^T$, where $C$ is the cofactor matrix), and Cramer's Rule. Also practical for small matrices with many zeros.

Determinants and Volume

Why does this single number matter so much? One of the most satisfying answers is geometric: the absolute value of the determinant is the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the rows of $A$.

In two dimensions, the rows of a $2 \times 2$ matrix define two vectors. The area of the parallelogram they span is $|\det A|$. When the matrix is the identity, the two rows are the standard basis vectors, spanning a unit square with area $1$—matching $\det I = 1$. When the rows are linearly dependent (one row is a multiple of the other), they collapse to a line, the parallelogram has zero area, and $\det A = 0$.

The sign of the determinant captures orientation: positive if the rows go "counterclockwise," negative if "clockwise." Row exchanges flip orientation, which is exactly Property 2.

This geometric picture extends cleanly to $n$ dimensions. The $n$ rows of an $n \times n$ matrix define the edges of an $n$-dimensional box. Its volume is $|\det A|$.

$|\det A|$ = volume of the box whose edges are the rows of $A$

$\det A = 0$ means the rows lie in a lower-dimensional subspace
