Solving $Ax=b$: Solvability and the Complete Solution

When analyzing linear systems, a fundamental question arises: when does $Ax=b$ have solutions x, and how can we describe those solutions? In order to find all solutions to $Ax=b$ we first check that the equation is solvable, then find a particular solution.

1. Solvability Conditions on b

Before calculating a solution, we must ensure one actually exists. We know that $Ax=b$ is solvable exactly when b is in the column space $C(A)$.

One way to find out whether $Ax=b$ is solvable is to use elimination on the augmented matrix. The process of elimination reveals the underlying dependencies between the equations. If a combination of the rows of A gives the zero row, then the same combination of the entries of b must equal zero. If a row of A is completely eliminated, so is the corresponding entry in b. If these conditions are not met, the system has no solution.

2. Finding a Particular Solution ($x_p$)

Once we know the system is solvable, our next step is to find just one single solution that satisfies the equation. One way to find a particular solution to the equation $Ax=b$ is to set all free variables to zero, then solve for the pivot variables.

The Particular Solution Algorithm
  1. Perform elimination to find the reduced row echelon form (RREF).
  2. Identify the free variables (columns without pivots) and the pivot variables (columns with pivots).
  3. Set all free variables to $0$.
  4. Solve the remaining simplified equations for the pivot variables.

3. The Complete Solution ($x_{complete}$)

A particular solution gives us a starting point, but it may not be the only solution. We get the complete solution of the equation by adding the particular solution to all the vectors in the nullspace.

Structure of the Complete Solution

The general solution to $Ax=b$ is given by $x_{complete}=x_{p}+x_{n}$, where $x_{n}$ is a generic vector in the nullspace.

$$ x_{complete} = x_p + c_1 s_1 + c_2 s_2 + \dots + c_k s_k $$

To see this, we add $Ax_{p}=b$ to $Ax_{n}=0$ and get $A(x_{p}+x_{n})=b$ for every vector $x_{n}$ in the nullspace.

The nullspace component, $x_n$, is constructed by finding all combinations of the special solutions to the equation $Ax=0$. Because $Ax_n$ always evaluates to $0$, adding it to our particular solution does not change the right-hand side $b$.

4. The Big Picture: Rank and Solutions

The rank of a matrix equals the number of pivots of that matrix. The rank ($r$), compared to the number of rows ($m$) and columns ($n$), dictates the entire behavior of the system's solutions. If A is an m by n matrix of rank r, we know $r \le m$ and $r \le n$.

Full Column Rank ($r=n$)

If $r=n$ then from the previous lecture we know that the nullspace has dimension $n-r=0$ and contains only the zero vector. There are no free variables or special solutions. Because the nullspace is empty, there is no $x_n$ to add. Consequently, if $Ax=b$ has a solution, it is unique; there is either 0 or 1 solution.

Full Row Rank ($r=m$)

If $r=m$ then the reduced matrix $R=\begin{bmatrix}I & F\end{bmatrix}$ has no rows of zeros and so there are no requirements for the entries of b to satisfy. The equation $Ax=b$ is solvable for every b. Because there are $n-m$ free variables, there are $n-m$ special solutions to $Ax=0$.

Full Row and Column Rank ($r=m=n$)

If $r=m=n$ is the number of pivots of A, then A is an invertible square matrix and R is the identity matrix. The nullspace has dimension zero, and $Ax=b$ has a unique solution for every b in $\mathbb{R}^{m}$.

Summary Table of Rank Outcomes

If R is in row reduced form with pivot columns first (rref), the table below summarizes our results.

Rank Condition RREF Shape ($R$) # Solutions to $Ax=b$
$r=m=n$ $I$ 1
$r=n \lt m$ $\begin{bmatrix}I \\ 0\end{bmatrix}$ 0 or 1
$r=m \lt n$ $\begin{bmatrix}I & F\end{bmatrix}$ infinitely many
$r \lt m$, $r \lt n$ $\begin{bmatrix}I & F \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix}$ 0 or infinitely many

5. A Complete Solved Example

Let's walk through finding the complete solution for a specific system $Ax=b$. We will use the following $3 \times 4$ matrix $A$ and a right-hand side vector $b$:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \\ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{bmatrix}, \quad b = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} $$

Notice that the third row of $A$ is the sum of its first and second rows. For the system to be solvable, the same combination must apply to $b$: we need $b_3 = b_1 + b_2$. Checking our vector, $6 = 1 + 5$, so the condition is satisfied and a solution exists.

Step 1: Find the Particular Solution ($x_p$)

We set the free variables to zero. In this matrix, columns 2 and 4 have no pivots, so our free variables are $x_2 = 0$ and $x_4 = 0$. This simplifies our system to just the pivot variables:

$$ \begin{matrix} x_1 + 2x_3 = 1 \\ 2x_3 = 3 \end{matrix} $$

Solving this via back substitution yields $x_3 = 3/2$ and $x_1 = -2$. Putting it all together, our particular solution is:

$$ x_p = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 3/2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 2: Find the Null Space ($x_n$)

The null space consists of all special solutions to $Ax=0$. By setting one free variable to $1$ and the others to $0$, we can solve for the two special solutions in this system:

$$ s_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad s_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Step 3: Combine for the Complete Solution

The general solution to $Ax=b$ is given by $x_{complete} = x_p + x_n$. We combine our particular solution with all possible linear combinations of our special solutions:

Complete Solution
$$ x_{complete} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \\ 3/2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + c_1 \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + c_2 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

6. The Geometric Picture: A Shifted Plane

To truly grasp linear algebra, it helps to visualize where these solutions live. In our example, there are four unknowns, meaning our vectors exist in $\mathbb{R}^4$.

First, consider the null space, $x_n$. Because there are two free variables (and two special solutions), all linear combinations of those vectors form a two-dimensional subspace—a flat plane that passes precisely through the origin (the zero vector).

Now, what happens when we add the particular solution $x_p$ to create the complete solution? We are taking that entire two-dimensional plane and shifting it away from the origin by the specific vector $x_p$.

Not a Subspace

Does the set of solutions to $Ax=b$ form a subspace? No.

Because the new plane has been shifted, it no longer contains the zero vector (unless $b=0$). The complete solutions to $Ax=b$ form a plane parallel to the null space, anchored at $x_p$, creating a flat geometric structure that is explicitly not a formal subspace.
