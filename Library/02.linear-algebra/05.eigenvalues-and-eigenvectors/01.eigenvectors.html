Eigenvectors: Directions That Do Not Change

Most vectors change direction when multiplied by a matrix. But a few special directions remain on the same line after transformation. These are the eigenvectors.

If a nonzero vector \(x\) satisfies

\[ Ax = \lambda x \]

then \(x\) is an eigenvector and \(\lambda\) is its eigenvalue.

The matrix may stretch, shrink, flip, or preserve the vector, but the direction itself survives.

  • \(\lambda > 1\): stretching
  • \(0 < \lambda < 1\): shrinking
  • \(\lambda < 0\): reversal
  • \(\lambda = 1\): unchanged
  • \(\lambda = 0\): sent to the nullspace

Let's pause for a second on \(\lambda = 0\). If a matrix has an eigenvalue of zero, it means \(Ax = 0x\), or simply \(Ax = 0\).

If \(\lambda = 0\) is an eigenvalue, the matrix \(A\) must be singular. The eigenvectors corresponding to \(\lambda = 0\) are exactly the non-zero vectors that make up the null space of \(A\).

Why Repeated Multiplication Reveals Eigenvectors

The true importance of eigenvectors appears in repeated powers:

\[ A,\; A^2,\; A^3,\; \dots,\; A^{100} \]

If \(Ax = \lambda x\), then

\[ A^k x = \lambda^k x \]
Key Insight

The direction survives every multiplication. Only the scale changes. This is the foundation of steady states and dominant modes.

Example: A Matrix Approaching Steady State

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 \\ 0.2 & 0.7 \end{bmatrix} \]

As the powers increase, the columns converge:

\[ A^{100} \approx \begin{bmatrix} 0.6 & 0.6 \\ 0.4 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \]

This happens because one eigenvalue remains dominant while the other decays.

Matrix Power: A0 (I)

Geometric Examples

Transformation Eigenvalues Meaning
Projection \(1,0\) one direction survives, one vanishes
Reflection \(1,-1\) one preserved, one reversed
Rotation \(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta}\) complex rotational modes

Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\). A non-zero vector \(\vec{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n\) is an eigenvector of \(A\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\in\mathbb{R}\) if

\[ A\vec{x}=\lambda\vec{x} \]

Equivalently, \(\vec{x}\) spans a one-dimensional subspace invariant under \(A\), i.e. \(A\vec{x}\in\text{span}(\vec{x})\).

Finding Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Start with the eigenvalue equation \(A\vec{x}=\lambda\vec{x}\), where \(\vec{x}\neq\vec{0}\). Move everything to one side:

\[ (A-\lambda I)\vec{x}=\vec{0} \]

Now observe: this is a homogeneous system with a nonzero solution. Such a solution exists only when \(A-\lambda I\) is singular.

Key condition

Therefore, eigenvalues are exactly the values of \(\lambda\) for which

\[ \det(A-\lambda I)=0 \]

This determinant is called the characteristic polynomial.

The determinant condition \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\) tells us which values of \(\lambda\) are eigenvalues. Once those are found, we substitute each one back into \((A-\lambda I)\vec{x}=\vec{0}\) to compute the corresponding eigenvectors.

This leads to a simple and systematic 3-step algorithm.

3-Step Algorithm
  1. Compute \(\det(A-\lambda I)\). This gives the characteristic polynomial.
  2. Solve \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\) to get the eigenvalues.
  3. For each eigenvalue, solve \((A-\lambda I)\vec{x}=\vec{0}\) to get eigenvectors.
Worked Example

Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of

\[ A= \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1\\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 1: Characteristic polynomial

\[ \det(A-\lambda I)= \begin{vmatrix} 4-\lambda & 1\\ 2 & 3-\lambda \end{vmatrix} =\lambda^2-7\lambda+10 \]

Step 2: Eigenvalues

\[ \lambda^2-7\lambda+10=0 \Rightarrow (\lambda-5)(\lambda-2)=0 \]

So the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1=5\) and \(\lambda_2=2\).

Step 3: Eigenvectors

For \(\lambda=5\):

\[ A-5I= \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1\\ 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1\\ 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x\\y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\0 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ -x+y=0 \Rightarrow y=x \]

Take \(x=1\), then \(y=1\), so one eigenvector is

\[ \vec{v}_1= \begin{bmatrix} 1\\1 \end{bmatrix} \]

For \(\lambda=2\):

\[ A-2I= \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x\\y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\0 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ 2x+y=0 \Rightarrow y=-2x \]

Take \(x=1\), then \(y=-2\), so one eigenvector is

\[ \vec{v}_2= \begin{bmatrix} 1\\-2 \end{bmatrix} \]

Built-in Checkpoints: The Trace and Determinant

When you calculate eigenvalues, how do you know if you made a mistake? Fortunately, every matrix comes with two built-in "sanity checks." By just looking at the original matrix, you can instantly know the sum and the product of its eigenvalues.

The Two Fundamental Identities

For any $n \times n$ matrix $A$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \dots, \lambda_n$:

  • The Sum Check: The sum of the eigenvalues equals the sum of the main diagonal (the Trace).
    $\sum \lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}(A)$
  • The Product Check: The product of the eigenvalues equals the Determinant.
    $\prod \lambda_i = \det(A)$

These aren't just lucky coincidences. They are an inevitable consequence of how polynomials work. The characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda)$ acts as a bridge, connecting the matrix entries on one side to the eigenvalues on the other. Let's see how this bridge is built.

The Intuition: The $2 \times 2$ Stepping Stone

The easiest way to see this connection is to build the characteristic polynomial for a generic $2 \times 2$ matrix from two completely different perspectives.

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$. Its eigenvalues are $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$.

Perspective 1: Building from the Matrix

$$ p(\lambda) = \det \begin{bmatrix} a-\lambda & b \\ c & d-\lambda \end{bmatrix} $$
$$ p(\lambda) = (a-\lambda)(d-\lambda) - bc $$
$$ p(\lambda) = \lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad-bc) $$

Look closely at those coefficients. $(a+d)$ is exactly the Trace. $(ad-bc)$ is exactly the Determinant. So, for any $2 \times 2$ matrix:

$$ p(\lambda) = \lambda^2 - \operatorname{tr}(A)\lambda + \det(A) $$

Perspective 2: Building from the Roots

If $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are the roots of this polynomial, basic algebra tells us we can also factor it as:

$$ p(\lambda) = (\lambda_1 - \lambda)(\lambda_2 - \lambda) $$
$$ p(\lambda) = \lambda^2 - (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)\lambda + (\lambda_1 \lambda_2) $$
The "Aha!" Moment

Both perspectives describe the exact same polynomial $p(\lambda)$. Therefore, their coefficients must match perfectly:

  • Comparing the $\lambda$ coefficients: $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \operatorname{tr}(A)$
  • Comparing the constant terms: $\lambda_1 \lambda_2 = \det(A)$

The General Proof ($n \times n$)

This exact same logic scales to a matrix of any size $n$. We write the characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda)$ in two ways:

$$ \text{Matrix Form: } \quad p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) $$
$$ \text{Root Form: } \quad p(\lambda) = (\lambda_1 - \lambda)(\lambda_2 - \lambda) \cdots (\lambda_n - \lambda) $$
1. Proving the Product equals the Determinant

How do you isolate the constant term of any polynomial? You simply plug in zero. Let's evaluate $p(0)$ for both forms.

  • Matrix Form: $p(0) = \det(A - 0I) = \mathbf{\det(A)}$
  • Root Form: $p(0) = (\lambda_1 - 0)(\lambda_2 - 0) \cdots (\lambda_n - 0) = \mathbf{\lambda_1 \lambda_2 \cdots \lambda_n}$

Since both equal $p(0)$, they must equal each other:

$$ \det(A) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i $$
2. Proving the Sum equals the Trace

To find the sum, we have to look at the coefficient of the $\lambda^{n-1}$ term.

From the Root Form:

If you multiply out $(\lambda_1 - \lambda)(\lambda_2 - \lambda) \cdots (\lambda_n - \lambda)$, you get $(-1)^n \lambda^n$ as the highest-order term. The next term, containing $\lambda^{n-1}$, is formed by multiplying $n-1$ of the $-\lambda$'s with exactly one of the $\lambda_i$ constants. Adding all these combinations together gives:

$$ (-1)^{n-1} (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \dots + \lambda_n) \lambda^{n-1} $$

From the Matrix Form:

Where do the $\lambda$'s live inside $\det(A - \lambda I)$? They only exist on the main diagonal.

$$ \begin{vmatrix} a_{11}-\lambda & a_{12} & \cdots \\ a_{21} & a_{22}-\lambda & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{vmatrix} $$

When calculating a large determinant, the only way to multiply enough $\lambda$'s together to reach $\lambda^n$ or $\lambda^{n-1}$ is to multiply the elements strictly down the main diagonal. Any other permutation in the determinant grabs too many off-diagonal numbers (which don't have $\lambda$), meaning the power of $\lambda$ falls to $n-2$ or lower.

So, the highest powers of $\lambda$ come exclusively from expanding that diagonal product:

$$ (a_{11}-\lambda)(a_{22}-\lambda) \cdots (a_{nn}-\lambda) $$

Expanding this product (just like we did with the roots) gives a $\lambda^{n-1}$ coefficient of:

$$ (-1)^{n-1} (a_{11} + a_{22} + \dots + a_{nn}) \lambda^{n-1} $$

The Conclusion:

We equate the $\lambda^{n-1}$ coefficients from both perspectives:

$$ (-1)^{n-1}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \dots + \lambda_n) = (-1)^{n-1}(a_{11} + a_{22} + \dots + a_{nn}) $$

Cancel out the $(-1)^{n-1}$, remember that the sum of the diagonal is the trace, and the proof is complete:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}(A) \quad \blacksquare $$

Consequences of \(A\vec{x}=\lambda\vec{x}\).

\[ A^k\vec{x}=\lambda^k\vec{x},\quad A^{-1}\vec{x}=\lambda^{-1}\vec{x},\quad (A+cI)\vec{x}=(\lambda+c)\vec{x} \]

A warning on matrix addition.

Because $(A+cI)\vec{x} = (\lambda+c)\vec{x}$, you might intuitively think that the eigenvalues of $A+B$ are just the eigenvalues of $A$ plus the eigenvalues of $B$. This is false.

Adding a multiple of the identity matrix ($cI$) works because the identity matrix doesn't change the direction of any vector—the eigenvectors of $A$ are identical to the eigenvectors of $A+cI$. But a general matrix $B$ will have entirely different eigenvectors. Because $A$ and $B$ do not share the same eigenvectors, you cannot simply add their eigenvalues together.

What if $A$ and $B$ share an eigenvector?

We saw that adding $cI$ works perfectly because the identity matrix shares all its eigenvectors with $A$. But what if a general matrix $B$ happens to share just one specific eigenvector $\vec{x}$ with $A$?

Suppose $A\vec{x} = \lambda_A\vec{x}$ and $B\vec{x} = \lambda_B\vec{x}$. If we apply the combined matrix $(A+B)$ to this specific shared vector:

$$ (A+B)\vec{x} = A\vec{x} + B\vec{x} = \lambda_A\vec{x} + \lambda_B\vec{x} = (\lambda_A + \lambda_B)\vec{x} $$

The algebra holds! For that shared direction only, the eigenvector survives the addition, and its new eigenvalue is exactly the sum: $\lambda_A + \lambda_B$.

This same logic applies effortlessly to matrix multiplication. If we apply the product $AB$ to that shared vector:

$$ (AB)\vec{x} = A(B\vec{x}) = A(\lambda_B\vec{x}) = \lambda_B(A\vec{x}) = \lambda_B(\lambda_A\vec{x}) = (\lambda_A\lambda_B)\vec{x} $$

This reveals a profound geometric truth: when matrices share an eigenvector, their transformations align perfectly along that specific axis, allowing their eigenvalues to simply add or multiply as if they were standard numbers.

What if the transformation is applied twice?

Suppose the same matrix acts on the eigenvector one more time:

\[ A(A\vec{x})=A(\lambda\vec{x}) \]

Since scalar multiplication can be pulled out,

\[ A(\lambda\vec{x})=\lambda A\vec{x} \]

Now use the eigenvalue equation once again:

\[ \lambda A\vec{x}=\lambda(\lambda\vec{x})=\lambda^2\vec{x} \]

So applying the same matrix twice gives

\[ A^2\vec{x}=\lambda^2\vec{x} \]

Repeated application and powers of eigenvalues.

Applying the same matrix repeatedly keeps the eigenvector direction unchanged, while the scaling factor gets multiplied each time. So after \(k\) applications,

\[ A^k\vec{x}=\lambda^k\vec{x} \]

This means every eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of \(A\) becomes \(\lambda^k\) for the matrix \(A^k\). So if the eigenvalues of \(A\) are \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_n\), then the eigenvalues of \(A^k\) are

\[ \lambda_1^k,\lambda_2^k,\dots,\lambda_n^k \]

Using the fact that the sum of eigenvalues equals the trace,

\[ \operatorname{tr}(A^k)=\lambda_1^k+\lambda_2^k+\cdots+\lambda_n^k \]

Diagonal and triangular matrices.

For any diagonal, lower triangular, or upper triangular matrix, the eigenvalues are exactly the entries on the main diagonal.

\[ D= \begin{bmatrix} d_{11} & 0 & 0\\ 0 & d_{22} & 0\\ 0 & 0 & d_{33} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad L= \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & 0 & 0\\ * & l_{22} & 0\\ * & * & l_{33} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad U= \begin{bmatrix} u_{11} & * & *\\ 0 & u_{22} & *\\ 0 & 0 & u_{33} \end{bmatrix} \]

Hence the eigenvalues are:

\[ D:\ d_{11},\,d_{22},\,d_{33} \qquad L:\ l_{11},\,l_{22},\,l_{33} \qquad U:\ u_{11},\,u_{22},\,u_{33} \]

Transpose and inverse.

If \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\), then \(\lambda\) is also an eigenvalue of \(A^T\). So \(A\) and \(A^T\) have exactly the same eigenvalues.

\[ \text{Eigenvalues of }A^T = \lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_n \]

If \(A\) is invertible, then the eigenvalues of \(A^{-1}\) are the reciprocals of the eigenvalues of \(A\).

\[ \text{Eigenvalues of }A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\lambda_1}, \frac{1}{\lambda_2}, \dots, \frac{1}{\lambda_n} \]

When Things Go Wrong: Complex and Defective Matrices

Not all matrices have a neat set of real, independent eigenvectors. There are two notable ways things can break down.

1. Complex Eigenvalues (The Rotation Matrix)

Consider a matrix \(Q\) that rotates every vector in the plane by \(90^\circ\). Are there any real vectors that come out parallel to where they started? No. Every vector changes direction.

If you solve \(\det(Q-\lambda I) = 0\) for a \(90^\circ\) rotation matrix, the equation becomes \(\lambda^2 + 1 = 0\). The eigenvalues are complex numbers (\(i\) and \(-i\)). Even for perfectly real matrices, extreme asymmetry (like rotations) forces complex eigenvalues into the picture.

2. Shortage of Eigenvectors (Defective Matrices)

Consider the following upper triangular matrix:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \]

Because it is triangular, we can read the eigenvalues right off the diagonal: \(\lambda_1 = 3\) and \(\lambda_2 = 3\). It is a repeated eigenvalue. If we subtract \(3I\) to find the eigenvectors, we get:

\[ A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

The only vectors in the null space of this matrix are multiples of \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\). There is no second, independent eigenvector. This matrix is degenerate (or defective).

This introduces the concepts of multiplicity:

Viewpoint What it measures Name
Algebra of \(\det(A-\lambda I)\) How many times \(\lambda\) appears as a root (e.g., \(\lambda=3\) appeared twice above) Algebraic multiplicity (AM)
Geometry of the eigenspace How many independent eigenvectors it produces (e.g., we only found one above) Geometric multiplicity (GM)
\[ 1 \le \mathrm{GM}(\lambda)\le \mathrm{AM}(\lambda) \]

When \(\mathrm{GM} < \mathrm{AM}\), the matrix is defective and does not have a complete set of independent eigenvectors.
