Projections

A projection begins with a restriction. You are given a vector $b$, but you are not allowed to use all directions in space. You are forced to stay within a smaller set—maybe just a line, maybe a plane.

Under that restriction, you try to get as close as possible to $b$. The result is not $b$ itself, but the best approximation that lives entirely inside the allowed directions. That vector is called the projection $p$. No matter what the subspace is, there is always a projection matrix $P$ that performs this operation:

$$p = Pb$$

To see what this really means, take $b = (2, 3, 4)$. Suppose you are only allowed to use the $xy$-plane. That means you have no way to represent any $z$-component. So, the best you can do is match the $x$ and $y$ parts and ignore the rest:

$$p_2 = (2, 3, 0)$$

The missing piece, $(0, 0, 4)$, cannot be expressed in the plane at all. It is not a mistake—it is the unavoidable error caused by the restriction. Now look at the same vector from the opposite perspective. If you are only allowed to move along the $z$-axis, then everything except the $z$-component disappears:

$$p_1 = (0, 0, 4)$$

These two parts, $p_1$ and $p_2$, are fundamentally different. One lies entirely inside the plane, the other entirely outside it. They do not overlap, and they do not interfere with each other. Together, they reconstruct the original vector:

$$b = p_1 + p_2$$

This decomposition happens because the $xy$-plane and the $z$-axis are orthogonal complements. One captures exactly what the other cannot. The matrices that produce these projections simply enforce the restriction by keeping the allowed components and discarding the rest. Because these two parts are independent and capture the entirety of the space, their matrices add up to the identity matrix:

$$P_1 + P_2 = I$$

Deriving the Projection onto a Line

Let a line pass through the origin in the direction of a vector $a$. We want to find the point $p$ on that line that is closest to our target vector $b$.

Geometrically, the projection $p$ lies entirely along the direction of $a$. We can capture this pure direction using a unit vector, $\hat{a} = \frac{a}{\|a\|}$. If we look at this through the lens of trigonometry, let $\theta$ be the angle between $b$ and $a$. The magnitude (or length) of the projection is exactly $\|b\| \cos \theta$, while the magnitude of the orthogonal error vector $e$ is given by $\|b\| \sin \theta$.

Therefore, the projected vector $p$ is simply the magnitude multiplying our unit direction:

$$p = (\|b\| \cos \theta) \hat{a}$$

While this trigonometric perspective is incredibly intuitive for 2D and 3D spaces, calculating angles in higher dimensions becomes cumbersome. Instead, linear algebra offers a more scalable and powerful tool: orthogonality.

Because $p$ must lie on the one-dimensional subspace created by $a$, it must be some scalar multiple of $a$. Let's call that unknown scalar $\hat{x}$:

$$p = \hat{x}a$$

For $p$ to be the absolute closest approximation of $b$, the error vector ($e = b - p$) must drop straight down at a right angle to the line. Because the error is perpendicular to the line $a$, their dot product must be zero:

$$a^T(b - p) = 0$$

Substitute $p = \hat{x}a$ into the equation and distribute the transpose:

$$a^T(b - \hat{x}a) = 0$$ $$a^Tb - \hat{x}(a^Ta) = 0$$
The Scalar Multiplier, Projection, & Matrix P
$$\hat{x} = \frac{a^T b}{a^T a}, \quad p = a \frac{a^T b}{a^T a}, \quad P = \frac{aa^T}{a^T a}$$

The numerator $a^T b$ measures how much $b$ aligns with $a$. The denominator $a^T a$ normalizes it. Notice that $aa^T$ is a column multiplied by a row, resulting in a full-scale $n \times n$ matrix of rank 1. This matrix keeps only the component of any vector that lies along $a$ and removes everything else.

Projection onto a Subspace $C(A)$

Why do we care about projecting onto larger subspaces? Because in the real world, we often encounter systems of equations $Ax = b$ that have too many equations and no exact solution. Vector $b$ simply isn't in the column space of $A$. So, we settle for the closest possible problem we can solve: $A\hat{x} = p$, where $p$ is the projection of $b$ onto the column space of $A$.

Now suppose we are allowed multiple directions $a_1, \dots, a_n$. Place them into the columns of a matrix $A$. Any vector in this higher-dimensional subspace has the form $A\hat{x}$. We want the specific combination $p = A\hat{x}$ that is closest to $b$.

The logic scales up perfectly. The condition for $p$ to be the closest point is that the error vector $e = b - p = b - A\hat{x}$ must be perfectly orthogonal (perpendicular) to the entire column space $C(A)$. This means $e$ must be orthogonal to every single column vector of $A$.

Mathematically, if $e$ is orthogonal to the column space of $A$, then multiplying the transpose of $A$ by $e$ must equal zero:

$$A^T e = 0$$

This equation perfectly mirrors the Four Fundamental Subspaces of linear algebra. By stating $A^T e = 0$, we are recognizing that the error vector $e$ lives exactly in the null space of $A^T$. As we know, the null space of $A^T$ is the orthogonal complement to the column space of $A$. The algebra perfectly matches our geometric intuition!

Substitute our error definition $e = b - A\hat{x}$ into the equation:

$$A^T(b - A\hat{x}) = 0$$

Distributing $A^T$ gives us the most important equation in least-squares approximations:

The Normal Equation
$$A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$$

Instead of trying to solve $Ax = b$, we solve for the best approximation by enforcing orthogonality of the error. From this, everything else follows:

$$\hat{x} = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$$ $$p = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$$

Factoring out $b$ reveals the $n$-dimensional projection matrix:

$$P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$$

A word of caution: Looking at $(A^T A)^{-1}$, it is tempting to use the matrix inverse product rule and split it into $A^{-1}(A^T)^{-1}$. If we did that, the entire projection formula would collapse to $A A^{-1} (A^T)^{-1} A^T = I$. Why can't we do this? Because $A$ is typically a tall, rectangular matrix—it does not have an inverse! We are forced to leave it as $(A^T A)^{-1}$.

The only exception is if $A$ is a nice, square, invertible matrix. But if $A$ is square and invertible, its columns span the entire space $\mathbb{R}^n$. In that case, $b$ is already in the column space, and the projection onto the entire space is simply the identity matrix $P = I$.

Properties of Projection Matrices

Whether projecting onto a single line or an $n$-dimensional subspace, projection matrices $P$ share two foundational properties:

  1. Symmetry ($P^T = P$): If you take the transpose of $P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$, the outer matrices $A$ and $A^T$ swap places and transpose, while the symmetric inner core $(A^TA)^{-1}$ remains unchanged. The matrix exactly equals its own transpose.
  2. Idempotence ($P^2 = P$): Geometrically, what happens if you apply a projection twice? The first projection pulls your vector down onto the subspace. Since the vector is now inside the subspace, a second projection leaves it exactly where it is. Algebraically, multiplying $P$ by itself yields:
    $$P^2 = \left[A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T\right] \left[A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T\right]$$
    The $A^T A$ in the middle multiplies against its own inverse, $(A^T A)^{-1}$, collapsing into the identity matrix. The expression immediately simplifies right back to $P$.

Application: Fitting a Line (Least Squares)

When would we actually need to project onto a subspace? The most common application occurs when fitting a theoretical model to messy, real-world data points that don't perfectly align.

Suppose we are given three data points in the format $(t, b)$: $(1, 1)$, $(2, 2)$, and $(3, 2)$. We want to fit them with the best possible straight line governed by the equation $C + Dt = b$. Let's plug in our points to see the system of equations:

$$C + 1D = 1$$ $$C + 2D = 2$$ $$C + 3D = 2$$

These three points do not lie on a single line, so this system has no exact solution. Let's extract the matrix $A$, the unknown vector $x$, and the right-hand side $b$:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad x = \begin{bmatrix} C \\ D \end{bmatrix}, \quad b = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 1: Calculate $A^T A$ and $A^T b$

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{bmatrix}$$ $$A^T b = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 2: Solve the Normal Equation ($A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$)

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} C \\ D \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix}$$

This translates to two equations. Let's multiply the first equation by 2 to make elimination easy:

  1. $6C + 12D = 10$
  2. $6C + 14D = 11$

Subtracting the first from the second gives $2D = 1$, so $D = 1/2$. Substitute $D$ back into the first equation: $3C + 6(1/2) = 5 \implies 3C = 2 \implies$ $C = 2/3$.

Our optimal coefficients are $\hat{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 2/3 \\ 1/2 \end{bmatrix}$. The best-fit line is $b = 2/3 + 1/2 t$. The vector $\hat{x}$ dictates exactly how to combine the columns of $A$ to find the projected vector $p$ that minimizes the error for our data points.

The Projection Matrix: Full Rank vs. Redundant Data

To project a vector $b$ onto the column space of $A$, we rely heavily on the projection matrix formula $P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$. However, this formula has a strict prerequisite: $A^T A$ must be invertible.

The Ideal Case (Full Column Rank): The matrix $A^T A$ is guaranteed to be invertible precisely when the columns of $A$ are linearly independent. If we assume there is some vector $x$ in its null space such that $A^T A x = 0$, we can multiply both sides by $x^T$:

$$x^T A^T A x = 0$$

Using the rules of transposes, $(Ax)^T = x^T A^T$, which simplifies the equation to:

$$(Ax)^T (Ax) = \|Ax\|^2 = 0$$

Because the length squared of a vector is zero only if the vector itself is zero, $Ax = 0$. If $A$'s columns are independent, the only solution to $Ax = 0$ is $x = 0$. Therefore, $A^T A$ has no null space and is safely invertible.

The Redundant Case (Fat Matrices and Rank Deficiency): What happens if $A$ has more columns than rows? This creates a "fat" matrix. When you have more vectors than available dimensions, the columns are mathematically guaranteed to be linearly dependent. (The same applies if $A$ is a tall matrix but includes a column that is just a copy or multiple of another).

In these cases, $A$ provides redundant information and is not full column rank. If you attempt to compute $A^T A$, the resulting matrix will have a determinant of exactly zero. It is singular and has no inverse. The standard projection formula completely fails because it demands a true, independent basis.

Finding the Projection Anyway: Even if $A$ is fat or has redundant columns, projecting onto its column space is still a mathematically valid goal. To find $P$, you must bypass the failed inverse using one of two methods:

  • Prune the Columns: Perform elimination to identify the independent pivot columns. Simply discard the extra, dependent columns to create a new matrix, $A_{basis}$. Since $A_{basis}$ now has full column rank, you can safely calculate $P = A_{basis}(A_{basis}^T A_{basis})^{-1} A_{basis}^T$.
  • Use the Pseudoinverse: If you don't want to manually strip away data, you can replace the standard inverse with the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse ($A^+$). The projection formula gracefully simplifies to $P = A A^+$, which automatically handles fat or singular matrices without breaking.
