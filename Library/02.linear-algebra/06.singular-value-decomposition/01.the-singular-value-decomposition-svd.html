The One-Line SVD Mental Model

SVD finds the input directions that remain orthogonal after transformation, and expresses the matrix as exactly three operations: rotation → scaling → rotation.

Why Eigenvectors Are Not Enough

When you study a square, symmetric matrix $S$, eigen-decomposition is perfect. You get $S = Q \Lambda Q^T$: one orthogonal matrix $Q$ to change basis, one diagonal matrix $\Lambda$ to scale, and then $Q^T$ to undo the rotation. The same basis works on both sides. Life is beautiful.

But what happens when $A$ is rectangular — say $m \times n$ with $m \neq n$? Or even square but asymmetric? Then eigenvectors either do not exist (no real eigenvalues), or they exist but are not orthogonal, so we cannot use the same geometric story.

The key obstruction is this: an eigenvector satisfies $Av = \lambda v$, meaning the output must live in the same space as the input. If $A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ with $m \neq n$, a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ cannot equal a scaled vector in $\mathbb{R}^m$. The concept collapses.

The SVD resolves this by relaxing the requirement. Instead of insisting $Av = \lambda v$ (same vector, same space), we ask for:

$$A v_i = \sigma_i\, u_i$$

Here $v_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an input direction, $u_i \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is an output direction, and $\sigma_i \geq 0$ is the scaling factor called a singular value. No requirement that $v_i = u_i$; they may live in entirely different spaces. This simple generalization unlocks the entire theory.

Five Ways to Discover the SVD

The SVD can be discovered through many independent routes. Each one illuminates a different facet of the same truth. We will walk all five, then unify them.

1. The Hypothesis: What If Special Directions Stay Orthogonal?

Here is a bold guess: suppose there is some orthonormal set of input directions $\{v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n\}$ such that after applying $A$, their images $\{Av_1, Av_2, \dots\}$ are still orthogonal to each other — just scaled and rotated.

Why is this a reasonable guess? Orthogonality is the only structure that survives rotations cleanly — so if anything is preserved under a linear map, orthogonality is the best candidate. A random matrix certainly can destroy orthogonality — perpendicular inputs can become non-perpendicular outputs. But we are hoping that with the right choice of inputs, perpendicularity survives the transformation.

Let us trace what this hypothesis implies. Suppose $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$ and $Av_j = \sigma_j u_j$, where $u_i, u_j$ are unit vectors with $u_i \perp u_j$. Take the dot product of $Av_i$ and $Av_j$:

$$(Av_i)^T (Av_j) = v_i^T A^T A v_j = 0$$

This means $v_i$ and $v_j$ must be eigenvectors of $A^T A$ — the matrix that measures inner products of outputs. If $v_i \neq v_j$ with different eigenvalues, eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix are automatically perpendicular. So the hypothesis is consistent: choose the $v_i$ to be eigenvectors of $A^T A$, and the outputs $Av_i$ will automatically come out orthogonal.

The scaling factors follow immediately. If $A^T A v_i = \lambda_i v_i$, then $\|Av_i\|^2 = v_i^T A^T A v_i = \lambda_i \|v_i\|^2 = \lambda_i$. So $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$. This is where singular values come from.

2. The Gram Matrix: $A^T A$ and $A A^T$ Share Eigenvalues

There is something striking if you look at the two Gram matrices associated with $A$. The row Gram matrix is $A^T A$ (an $n \times n$ matrix), and the column Gram matrix is $A A^T$ (an $m \times m$ matrix). These are different sizes. Yet they share all nonzero eigenvalues.

Why? Suppose $A^T A v = \lambda v$ with $\lambda \neq 0$ and $v \neq 0$. Multiply both sides on the left by $A$:

$$A (A^T A v) = \lambda (A v) \implies (A A^T)(Av) = \lambda (Av)$$

So $Av$ is an eigenvector of $A A^T$ with the same eigenvalue $\lambda$, provided $Av \neq 0$. The same argument works in reverse, from $AA^T$ back to $A^T A$.

So the nonzero eigenvalues are identical. Their eigenvectors are related by:

$$u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A v_i, \qquad v_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A^T u_i$$

Since $A^T A$ and $A A^T$ are both symmetric positive semidefinite, their eigenvectors can be chosen to be orthonormal — and that gives us the right singular vectors ($V$) and left singular vectors ($U$) directly.

3. Maximum Stretch: Finding the Direction $A$ Pulls Hardest

Here is the most geometric and perhaps the most natural way to think about it. You have a matrix $A$. You want to know: which unit vector does $A$ stretch the most?

$$\text{maximize} \quad \|Av\|^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|v\| = 1$$

Expand the objective:

$$\|Av\|^2 = (Av)^T(Av) = v^T A^T A v$$

Maximizing a quadratic form $v^T (A^TA) v$ over the unit sphere always gives the answer: the maximum is $\lambda_{\max}$, achieved when $v$ is the corresponding eigenvector of $A^TA$. So the direction of maximum stretch is the top eigenvector, and the maximum stretch is $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{\lambda_{\max}}$.

Now deflate: restrict to the subspace orthogonal to the previous directions, ensuring the next vector captures a new independent direction of stretch. Continue until all singular values are found.

4. Orthogonal-Matrix Motivation: When Eigenvectors Don't Exist

For a general square matrix we decompose it using eigenvectors. But here is a puzzle: orthogonal matrices don't necessarily have any real eigenvectors at all. Think of a pure rotation $R$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ by some angle $\theta \neq 0, \pi$. Every vector gets rotated — none stays in the same direction. So $Rv = \lambda v$ has no real solution.

Yet a rotation is not mysterious at all. We can describe it beautifully: it takes one orthonormal set of vectors and turns it into another orthonormal set.

The SVD is the direct generalization of this idea to any matrix. Even when $A$ is rectangular, or singular, or asymmetric, it takes some orthonormal set in the input space and maps it to another orthonormal set in the output space — with scalings in between.

5. Basis Matching: Connecting Two Orthogonal Sets

Before applying $A$, we represent points using one basis. After applying $A$, we represent the results using another basis. These two bases live in different spaces — $\mathbb{R}^n$ before and $\mathbb{R}^m$ after — and there is no reason they should be the same.

What the SVD does is precisely connect these two orthogonal sets. The columns of $V$ are the orthonormal basis on the input side. The columns of $U$ are the orthonormal basis on the output side. The singular values $\Sigma$ tell you the scaling that connects each input direction to its corresponding output direction.

Compression Checkpoint

Let’s pause and compress what just happened across these five views:

  • $A^T A$ gives the perfect input directions ($V$).
  • $A A^T$ gives the perfect output directions ($U$).
  • They share the same eigenvalues, meaning the stretch factor ($\sigma$) linking them is identical.

The Formal Statement and Shapes of $U$, $\Sigma$, $V$

Let $A$ be any real $m \times n$ matrix of rank $r$. The full SVD writes:

$$\boxed{A = U \Sigma V^T}$$

where:

  • $U$ is $m \times m$, orthogonal ($U^T U = I_m$). Its columns $u_1, \dots, u_m$ are the left singular vectors.
  • $V$ is $n \times n$, orthogonal ($V^T V = I_n$). Its columns $v_1, \dots, v_n$ are the right singular vectors.
  • $\Sigma$ is $m \times n$ with $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ on its main diagonal and zeros elsewhere.

The Four Fundamental Subspaces, Revealed

The SVD is the definitive geometric answer to finding orthonormal bases for the four fundamental subspaces of a matrix. The columns of $V$ neatly partition the input space into the row space and the null space. The columns of $U$ partition the output space into the column space and the left null space.

The action of $A$ is now completely clear: $A$ maps the basis vectors of the row space directly to the basis vectors of the column space (scaled by the magnitude of $\sigma_i$), and it completely annihilates everything in the null space. We will explore the rigorous projections onto these spaces in the properties article.

How to Compute the SVD: Step-by-Step

The Standard Procedure

  1. Form $A^T A$ (an $n\times n$ symmetric positive semidefinite matrix).
  2. Find eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq 0$ of $A^T A$.
  3. Set singular values $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$.
  4. Find eigenvectors of $A^T A$ (orthonormalize within each repeated-eigenvalue subspace). These form the columns of $V$.
  5. Compute $u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A v_i$ for each $i$ with $\sigma_i > 0$. These are already unit vectors and automatically orthogonal.
  6. Complete $U$: for the zero singular values, choose $u_{r+1}, \dots, u_m$ to be any orthonormal basis for $N(A^T)$ (e.g., via Gram-Schmidt on the remaining directions).
Always compute $u_i$ from $u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} Av_i$ rather than from the eigenvectors of $AA^T$ directly. Eigenvectors are only defined up to sign; using this formula ensures $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$ holds with the correct sign to bridge the two matrices.

Worked Examples

Example 1: A Full-Rank 2×2 Matrix

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$, which has rank 2.

Step 1: Compute $A^T A$

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix}4&-3\\4&3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}4&4\\-3&3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}32&0\\0&18\end{bmatrix}$$

It came out diagonal — eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 32$, $\lambda_2 = 18$ are immediate. Singular values: $\sigma_1 = 4\sqrt{2}$, $\sigma_2 = 3\sqrt{2}$.

Step 2: Eigenvectors of $A^T A$ → columns of $V$

The eigenvectors are along $(1,0)^T$ and $(0,1)^T$:

$$V = \begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$$

Step 3: Compute $U$ via $u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}Av_i$

$$Av_1 = \begin{bmatrix}4&4\\-3&3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4\\-3\end{bmatrix}$$

So $u_1 = \frac{1}{4\sqrt{2}}(4,-3)^T$. Similarly for $u_2$, giving us the final matrices for $U$, $\Sigma$, and $V^T$.

Example 2: A Rank-1 Matrix — When a Whole Row Space Collapses

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix}4&3\\8&6\end{bmatrix}$. Notice row 2 = 2 × row 1. Rank 1.

The geometry

$A$ crushes all of $\mathbb{R}^2$ onto a single line — the column space is spanned by $(4,8)^T$, or after normalizing, $u_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(1,2)^T$. The row space is spanned by $(4,3)^T$, normalized to $v_1 = (0.8, 0.6)^T$.

Singular value

$$A^TA = \begin{bmatrix}80&60\\60&45\end{bmatrix}$$

Rank 1, so one eigenvalue is zero; the other is the trace $= 125$. Thus $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{125} = 5\sqrt{5}$, $\sigma_2 = 0$.

Full SVD

$$A = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}5\sqrt{5}&0\\0&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}0.8&0.6\\0.6&-0.8\end{bmatrix}$$
Example 3: A Rectangular 3×2 Matrix

Let $A = \begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$, a $3\times 2$ matrix of rank 2.

$A^T A$ is $2\times 2$

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix}1&1&0\\1&0&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_{1,2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{4}}{2}$... actually $(2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = 0$, so $\lambda = 3$ and $\lambda = 1$. Singular values: $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{3}$, $\sigma_2 = 1$.

Eigenvectors of $A^T A$ → $V$

For $\lambda=3$: $(A^TA - 3I)v = 0$ gives $v_1 = (1,1)^T/\sqrt{2}$. For $\lambda=1$: $v_2 = (1,-1)^T/\sqrt{2}$.

Left singular vectors

$$u_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}Av_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\1\end{bmatrix}, \quad u_2 = Av_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\-1\end{bmatrix}$$

We need a third $u_3$ orthogonal to both, spanning the left null space. By cross-product or Gram-Schmidt: $u_3 \propto (-1, 1, 1)$... normalize appropriately. The SVD is $3\times 3$ times $3\times 2$ times $2\times 2$.

The master identity
$$A = U\Sigma V^T$$

Eigendecomposition (when it exists): change basis → scale → return to the same basis.
SVD (always): change basis in $\mathbb{R}^n$ → scale → change to a different basis in $\mathbb{R}^m$.
