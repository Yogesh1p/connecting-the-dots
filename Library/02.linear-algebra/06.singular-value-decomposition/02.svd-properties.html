The Singular Value Decomposition is a highlight of linear algebra because it addresses the deepest flaws of standard eigendecomposition. When we try to diagonalize a matrix $A$ via $A = X\Lambda X^{-1}$, we face three major problems: the eigenvectors in $X$ are usually not orthogonal, there are often not enough of them (defective matrices), and $A$ is strictly required to be square.

The singular vectors of $A$ solve all those problems perfectly. By paying the price of managing two distinct sets of vectors ($u$'s and $v$'s), we unlock the definitive analytical mechanics of any matrix—square or rectangular.

Diagonalizing with Two Bases: $AV = U\Sigma$

Because the singular vectors are explicitly linked by the mapping equation $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$, we can express this column-by-column as a single matrix multiplication:

$$A \begin{bmatrix} | & & | \\ v_1 & \cdots & v_n \\ | & & | \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} | & & | \\ u_1 & \cdots & u_m \\ | & & | \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 & & \\ & \ddots & \\ & & \sigma_r \end{bmatrix}$$

Or simply, $AV = U\Sigma$. Because $V$ is an orthogonal matrix with orthonormal columns, its inverse is its transpose ($V^{-1} = V^T$). Multiplying both sides on the right by $V^T$ gives us the standard SVD formula: $A = U\Sigma V^T$.

This reveals that the SVD is ultimately just diagonalizing a matrix using two different orthonormal bases instead of one. This two-bases diagonalization often carries much more geometric and structural information than $A = X\Lambda X^{-1}$.

The Outer Product Expansion

If we carry out the multiplication $A = U\Sigma V^T$ by taking columns of $U\Sigma$ times rows of $V^T$, we produce a profound summation:

$$A = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T + \sigma_2 u_2 v_2^T + \cdots + \sigma_r u_r v_r^T$$

This splits $A$ into a combination of $r$ strictly rank-one matrices. Because the singular values are sorted in descending order ($\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \dots \geq \sigma_r > 0$), this expansion organizes the matrix into distinct layers of importance. The first term, $\sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T$, represents the single largest structural piece of $A$. This splitting is the critical foundation for data compression and low-rank approximations.

The Four Fundamental Subspaces

More than just orthogonality, the $u$'s and $v$'s give us the exact, definitive orthonormal bases for the four fundamental subspaces of any $m \times n$ matrix $A$ of rank $r$.

SubspaceBasis from SVDDimension
Row space $C(A^T)$$v_1, \dots, v_r$ (first $r$ columns of $V$)$r$
Null space $N(A)$$v_{r+1}, \dots, v_n$ (last $n-r$ columns of $V$)$n-r$
Column space $C(A)$$u_1, \dots, u_r$ (first $r$ columns of $U$)$r$
Left null space $N(A^T)$$u_{r+1}, \dots, u_m$ (last $m-r$ columns of $U$)$m-r$

Using these bases, the exact projection matrices onto these spaces become trivial. Letting $U_r$ be the first $r$ columns of $U$, the projection onto the column space is strictly $P_{C(A)} = U_r U_r^T$. Letting $V_r$ be the first $r$ columns of $V$, the projection onto the row space is $P_{C(A^T)} = V_r V_r^T$.

Singular Value Stability vs. Eigenvalue Instability

One of the greatest triumphs of the SVD is its unconditional numerical stability. Eigenvalues, by contrast, can be terrifyingly unstable when a matrix is not symmetric.

Consider a $4 \times 4$ strictly triangular matrix $A$ with zeros on the diagonal and some non-zero entries above it. Because it is triangular, its eigenvalues are exactly $0, 0, 0, 0$. Now, suppose we perturb the matrix by changing the bottom-left entry from $0$ to just $1/60,000$.

That tiny change of $1/60,000$ produces a massive jump in the eigenvalues. The four eigenvalues move from zero outward, spacing themselves onto a circle in the complex plane with a radius of $1/10$. This demonstrates serious instability when $A^TA$ is far from $AA^T$.

By contrast, singular values are completely stable. They do not change by more than the magnitude of the change in the matrix itself. If you perturb a matrix by $1/60,000$, the singular values will shift by at most $1/60,000$. They are the fixed, reliable DNA of the matrix.

Matrix Norms and the Pseudoinverse

Because the SVD separates geometry from magnitude, measuring a matrix collapses into simple arithmetic on the singular values.

  • Spectral (Operator) Norm: $\|A\|_2 = \sigma_1$. The absolute maximum stretch $A$ can apply to any unit vector.
  • Frobenius Norm: $\|A\|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2+\cdots+\sigma_r^2}$. The total "energy" of the matrix.
  • Nuclear Norm: $\|A\|_* = \sigma_1+\cdots+\sigma_r$. The sum of the stretch factors.

Furthermore, the SVD defines the ultimate generalized inverse. If $A$ is rectangular or singular, we define the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse as:

$$A^+ = V \Sigma^+ U^T$$

Here, $\Sigma^+$ is formed by replacing every strictly positive $\sigma_i$ with $1/\sigma_i$ and transposing. This pseudoinverse is mathematical magic: computing $x^+ = A^+ b$ automatically finds the exact least-squares solution for overdetermined systems, and selects the solution with the minimum Euclidean norm for underdetermined systems.

Polar Decomposition: $A = QS$

The SVD immediately yields the polar decomposition for any square $n \times n$ matrix:

$$A = U\Sigma V^T = (UV^T)(V\Sigma V^T) = QS$$

Here, $Q = UV^T$ is an orthogonal matrix, and $S = V\Sigma V^T$ is a symmetric positive semidefinite matrix. This proves that any physical deformation or linear map can be factored precisely into a pure structural strain ($S$) followed by a rigid rotation ($Q$).

Computing the SVD: The Numerical Reality

Analytically, we know the singular values are the square roots of the eigenvalues of $S = A^TA$, and $V$ contains its eigenvectors.

However, we never actually compute $A^TA$ in practice. Squaring a matrix squares its condition number (making it $\kappa(A)^2$), which ruthlessly destroys numerical precision on computers. Furthermore, solving $\det(A^TA - \lambda I) = 0$ involves massive polynomials, which is universally avoided in modern algorithms.

Instead, algorithms like those in LAPACK use a two-step approach that preserves numerical stability perfectly:

  1. We multiply $A$ on the left and right by a sequence of simple orthogonal matrices ($Q_1^T A Q_2$) to introduce zeros. Because orthogonal matrices don't change lengths, the singular values remain totally unchanged.
  2. This iterative process stops when we reach a bidiagonal matrix (non-zeros only on the main diagonal and the one directly above it).
  3. From the bidiagonal state, a highly specialized QR algorithm pushes the off-diagonal entries toward zero, finally leaving us with the pure diagonal matrix $\Sigma$, while the accumulated orthogonal matrices become $U$ and $V^T$.

The Matrix Norm: Why $\|A\| = \sigma_1$

The entry $\sigma_1$ is not just the "largest singular value." It is the precise answer to the most natural question you can ask about a matrix: how much can it stretch a vector?

$$\|A\|_2 = \max_{x \neq 0} \frac{\|Ax\|}{\|x\|} = \sigma_1$$

Proof. Write $A = U\Sigma V^T$. Multiplying by an orthogonal matrix preserves lengths: $\|Qx\| = \|x\|$ for any orthogonal $Q$, because $\|Qx\|^2 = x^T Q^T Q x = x^T x$. Applying this to both $U$ and $V^T$:

$$\|Ax\| = \|U \Sigma V^T x\| = \|\Sigma V^T x\| = \|\Sigma y\|$$

where $y = V^T x$ is just a change of coordinates — and since $V^T$ is orthogonal, $\|y\| = \|x\|$. Now $\Sigma y$ simply scales each coordinate: $(\Sigma y)_i = \sigma_i y_i$. So:

$$\frac{\|Ax\|}{\|x\|} = \frac{\|\Sigma y\|}{\|y\|} = \frac{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 y_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 y_2^2 + \cdots}}{\sqrt{y_1^2 + y_2^2 + \cdots}} \leq \frac{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2 (y_1^2 + y_2^2 + \cdots)}}{\sqrt{y_1^2 + y_2^2 + \cdots}} = \sigma_1$$

The upper bound $\sigma_1$ is achieved when $y = (1, 0, \ldots, 0)^T$, i.e., when $x = v_1$. At that point $Av_1 = \sigma_1 u_1$ and the ratio equals exactly $\sigma_1$. $\square$

A direct corollary is one of the most useful facts in matrix analysis: every eigenvalue satisfies $|\lambda| \leq \sigma_1$. If $Ax = \lambda x$ with $\|x\|=1$, then $|\lambda| = |\lambda|\|x\| = \|\lambda x\| = \|Ax\| \leq \sigma_1 \|x\| = \sigma_1$.

Two further inequalities drop out of the definition immediately. For any two compatible matrices:

$$\|A + B\| \leq \|A\| + \|B\| \qquad \text{(triangle inequality)}$$ $$\|AB\| \leq \|A\|\,\|B\| \qquad \text{(product inequality)}$$

The first follows from the triangle inequality for vectors: $\|(A+B)x\| \leq \|Ax\| + \|Bx\| \leq \|A\|\|x\| + \|B\|\|x\|$. Divide by $\|x\|$ and take the maximum. The second follows by applying the bound $\|Bx\| \leq \|B\|\|x\|$ first, then $\|A(Bx)\| \leq \|A\|\|Bx\|$.

What $\sigma_{\min}$ Measures: Distance to Singularity

The polar decomposition $A = QS$ has a beautiful side effect. The factor $Q = UV^T$ is not just an orthogonal matrix — it is the nearest orthogonal matrix to $A$:

$Q = UV^T$ minimizes $\|Q_0 - A\|$ over all orthogonal matrices $Q_0$.

This is the matrix analogue of the fact that $e^{i\theta}$ is the nearest point on the unit circle to the complex number $re^{i\theta}$, since the polar decomposition of $re^{i\theta}$ is $r \cdot e^{i\theta} = (\text{magnitude}) \cdot (\text{unit circle point})$.

Even more striking: $\sigma_{\min}$ is exactly the distance from $A$ to the nearest singular matrix. If $A = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$, then knocking out the last (smallest) piece $\sigma_r u_r v_r^T$ produces the closest rank-$(r-1)$ matrix to $A$, at a cost of exactly $\sigma_r$. By the Eckart–Young theorem, no other rank-$(r-1)$ matrix is closer.

In numerical practice, this gives singular values their second major job: diagnosing ill-conditioning. The ratio $\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ is the condition number $\kappa(A)$. When $\kappa(A)$ is large, $A$ is close to being singular, and solving $Ax = b$ will amplify any rounding error in $b$ by a factor of up to $\kappa(A)$.

The Complete Action of $A^+$: Projections

The pseudoinverse $A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$ does something geometrically precise. By choosing the SVD bases, we can track exactly what happens to every vector.

A matrix $A$ of rank $r$ partitions its input space $\mathbb{R}^n$ into the row space (dimension $r$) and the null space (dimension $n-r$), and its output space $\mathbb{R}^m$ into the column space (dimension $r$) and the left null space (dimension $m-r$). The pseudoinverse reverses the invertible part and kills the rest:

Input to $A^+$Output of $A^+$
$u_i$ (column space of $A$, $i \leq r$)$v_i / \sigma_i$ (row space of $A$)
$u_i$ (left null space of $A$, $i > r$)$\mathbf{0}$

Two products of $A$ and $A^+$ become projection matrices, not the identity:

$$A^+ A = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}_{\text{in row/null decomposition}} = \text{projection onto the row space of } A$$
$$A A^+ = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}_{\text{in col/left-null decomposition}} = \text{projection onto the column space of } A$$

These are orthogonal projections — the best possible. When $A$ is square and invertible, $r = m = n$ and both products become the identity, recovering the familiar $A^{-1}A = AA^{-1} = I$.

This makes the pseudoinverse the only "inverse" that is simultaneously:

  • Exact on the solvable part: for any $b$ in the column space, $A(A^+b) = b$.
  • Minimum-norm: among all $x$ solving the normal equations $A^TAx = A^Tb$, the solution $x^+ = A^+b$ is the unique one lying in the row space — i.e., the shortest one.
  • Least-squares: $x^+ = A^+b$ minimizes $\|Ax - b\|$ even when no exact solution exists.

These three properties together are the Moore–Penrose conditions, and $A^+ = V\Sigma^+U^T$ is the unique matrix satisfying all of them.

The Reverse Polar Decomposition: $A = KQ$

The polar decomposition $A = QS$ (stretch then rotate) has a twin. Inserting $UU^T = I$ instead:

$$A = U\Sigma V^T = (U\Sigma U^T)(UV^T) = KQ$$

Here $Q = UV^T$ is the same orthogonal matrix as before, but now $K = U\Sigma U^T$ is the positive semidefinite factor on the left. If $S$ was the symmetric square root of $A^TA$ (since $S^2 = V\Sigma^2 V^T = A^TA$), then $K$ is the symmetric square root of $AA^T$ (since $K^2 = U\Sigma^2 U^T = AA^T$). In mechanics, $A = QS$ represents stretch-then-rotate while $A = KQ$ represents rotate-then-stretch — two physically distinct ways to reach the same deformation $A$.

A Hidden Equivalence: SVD as Eigendecomposition

The SVD of $A$ is secretly equivalent to the eigendecomposition of one symmetric matrix. Form the $(m+n) \times (m+n)$ symmetric block matrix:

$$M = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & A \\ A^T & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This matrix has a beautiful eigenstructure. For each singular triple $(\sigma_i, u_i, v_i)$ of $A$, the vectors $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} u_i \\ v_i \end{bmatrix}$ and $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} u_i \\ -v_i \end{bmatrix}$ are eigenvectors of $M$ with eigenvalues $+\sigma_i$ and $-\sigma_i$ respectively. You can verify directly:

$$M \begin{bmatrix} u_i \\ v_i \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} Av_i \\ A^Tu_i \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_i u_i \\ \sigma_i v_i \end{bmatrix} = \sigma_i \begin{bmatrix} u_i \\ v_i \end{bmatrix}$$

So the SVD of any $m \times n$ matrix $A$ is completely equivalent to the eigendecomposition of this symmetric $(m+n)\times(m+n)$ matrix. The singular values of $A$ appear in $\pm$ pairs as eigenvalues of $M$. This is one of the deepest structural connections in matrix theory — it allows every theorem about symmetric eigenvalue problems to be translated directly into a theorem about singular values.
