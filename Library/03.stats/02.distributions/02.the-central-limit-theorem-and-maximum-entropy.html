The Central Limit Theorem: Order from Chaos

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is perhaps the most magical result in all of mathematics. It states that when independent random variables are added together, their properly normalized sum tends toward a normal distribution (a bell curve)—regardless of the original variables' underlying distribution.

Imagine a Galton Board: a vertical board with interleaved rows of pegs. When you drop a single marble, it bounces left and right chaotically, its final resting place entirely unpredictable. But drop 10,000 marbles, and they will inevitably form a perfect, smooth bell curve at the bottom. Whether you are summing the rolls of a die, the heights of people, or the noise in an electrical signal, if you add enough of them together, the Gaussian shape always emerges.

The Central Limit Theorem (Classical)

Let $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$ be a sequence of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables drawn from a population with expected value given by $\mu$ and finite variance given by $\sigma^2$.

As the sample size $n$ approaches infinity, the probability distribution of the standardized sample mean approaches a standard normal distribution:

$$ \sqrt{n}\left( \left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) - \mu \right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) $$

1. The Anatomy of the Normal Distribution

The culmination of the CLT is the Probability Density Function (PDF) of the Normal Distribution. You have likely seen this imposing equation before. Let's look at it closely:

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2} $$

For many students, this formula feels pulled from thin air. Why $e$? Why is the variable squared? And most mysteriously, where does the $\sqrt{2\pi}$ come from in a problem that seems to have nothing to do with circles?

To understand why nature prefers this exact mathematical form, we must step outside traditional statistics and look at the universe through the lens of Information Theory.

2. Maximum Entropy: Making No Assumptions

In information theory, Shannon Entropy measures the amount of uncertainty, surprise, or "chaos" in a probability distribution. The principle of maximum entropy states that the probability distribution which best represents the current state of knowledge is the one with the largest entropy, subject to known constraints. In simpler terms: it is the mathematical embodiment of "I don't know anything else."

Differential Entropy

For a continuous probability density function $f(x)$, the differential entropy $H(f)$ is defined as:

$$ H(f) = -\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \ln f(x) \, dx $$

Suppose we are observing our marbles falling in the Galton board. We only know two physical facts about where they land:

  1. They have a specific mean (average resting place), $\mu$.
  2. They have a specific finite variance (spread), $\sigma^2$.

If we want to assume absolutely nothing else about the data, we must find the probability distribution $f(x)$ that maximizes the entropy $H(f)$ without breaking our two rules.

3. The Derivation: Step-by-Step

We have an optimization problem. We want to maximize $H(f)$, but we have three strict mathematical constraints that our function $f(x)$ must obey:

$$ \begin{align*} 1. \quad &\int f(x) \, dx = 1 \quad \text{(Total probability must equal 100%)} \\ 2. \quad &\int x f(x) \, dx = \mu \quad \text{(The mean constraint)} \\ 3. \quad &\int (x-\mu)^2 f(x) \, dx = \sigma^2 \quad \text{(The variance constraint)} \end{align*} $$

Step 3.1: Enter Lagrange Multipliers

How do we maximize a function while forcing it to follow rules? We use a technique called Lagrange Multipliers. The idea is brilliant but simple: we combine our main goal (entropy) and our rules (constraints) into one "master equation" called the Lagrangian ($\mathcal{L}$).

We do this by setting our constraints to equal zero, multiplying each by an unknown constant (the multipliers $\lambda_0, \lambda_1, \lambda_2$), and subtracting them from our entropy equation:

$$ \mathcal{L} = -\int f(x)\ln f(x) dx - \lambda_0 \left(\int f(x) dx - 1\right) - \lambda_1 \left(\int x f(x) dx - \mu\right) - \lambda_2 \left(\int (x-\mu)^2 f(x) dx - \sigma^2\right) $$

Because integrals are linear, we can pull everything into a single integral to look at the function inside (the integrand):

$$ \text{Integrand} = -f(x)\ln f(x) - \lambda_0 f(x) - \lambda_1 x f(x) - \lambda_2 (x-\mu)^2 f(x) $$

Step 3.2: The Calculus of Variations

In regular calculus, to find the maximum of a curve, you take the derivative and set it to zero. But here, we aren't looking for a single point $x$; we are looking for an entire function $f(x)$ that maximizes our master equation. This requires the Calculus of Variations.

Fortunately, because our integrand only depends on $f(x)$ and not its derivatives, the rule (known as the Euler-Lagrange equation) simplifies beautifully. We just treat $f(x)$ as a standard variable, take the partial derivative of the integrand with respect to $f(x)$, and set it to zero:

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial f(x)} \left[ -f(x)\ln f(x) - \lambda_0 f(x) - \lambda_1 x f(x) - \lambda_2(x-\mu)^2 f(x) \right] = 0 $$

Using the product rule on $-f(x)\ln f(x)$ gives us $-\ln f(x) - 1$. The $f(x)$ drops out of the other terms, leaving:

$$ -\ln f(x) - 1 - \lambda_0 - \lambda_1 x - \lambda_2(x-\mu)^2 = 0 $$

Step 3.3: The Exponential Emerges

Now, we simply solve for $f(x)$. First, isolate the natural log:

$$ \ln f(x) = -1 - \lambda_0 - \lambda_1 x - \lambda_2 (x-\mu)^2 $$

To get rid of the natural log, we raise $e$ to the power of both sides. And just like that, the famous bell curve shape appears!

$$ f(x) = e^{-1 - \lambda_0 - \lambda_1 x - \lambda_2 (x-\mu)^2} $$

Step 3.4: Solving for the Constants

We know variance is perfectly symmetrical around the mean $\mu$. For our resulting function to be symmetrical, the linear term $\lambda_1 x$ must be zero, meaning $\lambda_1 = 0$. We can also group the constant terms $e^{-1 - \lambda_0}$ into a single normalizing constant, $C$.

$$ f(x) = C \cdot e^{-\lambda_2 (x-\mu)^2} $$

Now we apply our original constraints to find $C$ and $\lambda_2$. First, the total probability must equal 1 ($\int f(x) dx = 1$). To integrate $e^{-x^2}$, we use the famous Gaussian integral, which mathematically requires squaring the function into two dimensions and converting it to polar coordinates. This conversion to polar coordinates introduces the circle area relation, explaining exactly why $\pi$ appears in our statistics!

Using the Gaussian integral result $\int e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}$, we enforce our constraint:

$$ \int C e^{-\lambda_2 (x-\mu)^2} dx = 1 \implies C \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{\lambda_2}} = 1 \implies C = \sqrt{\frac{\lambda_2}{\pi}} $$

Next, we apply the variance constraint $\int (x-\mu)^2 f(x) dx = \sigma^2$, which evaluates to:

$$ \frac{1}{2\lambda_2} = \sigma^2 \implies \lambda_2 = \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} $$

Step 3.5: The Final Equation

Substitute $\lambda_2 = \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}$ back into our equation for $C$:

$$ C = \sqrt{\frac{1/(2\sigma^2)}{\pi}} = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} $$

Putting it all together, we arrive at the exact probability density function for the Normal Distribution:

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} $$

Conclusion: The normal distribution isn't just an arbitrary curve; it is the most unbiased, maximum-entropy distribution possible for a given mean and variance. As independent variables are added, convolution blends their underlying structures away, driving the system inevitably toward maximum entropy.
