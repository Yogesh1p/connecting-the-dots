The Foundation of Hypothesis Testing

At its core, hypothesis testing is a formal system for deciding whether an observed pattern is real, or just a coincidence of random sampling. We always start by pitting two opposing views against each other:

  • Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): The baseline assumption. There is no relationship, no difference, or no effect. (e.g., There is no relationship between gender and highest educational attainment).
  • Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$): The claim we are trying to prove. There is a relationship, difference, or effect.
The Intuition of Significance Level ($\alpha$)

Suppose we set our significance level ($\alpha$) to 5%. What does this actually mean?

Even if the Null Hypothesis is perfectly true, random variation means that extreme samples can still happen. A 5% significance level means we are drawing a line in the sand: if our sample yields a result so extreme that it has less than a 5% chance of occurring in a world where $H_0$ is true, we reject $H_0$.

Yes, there is a small chance we are making a mistake (rejecting a true null), but we are mathematically ensuring we are only wrong 5% of the time with this decision rule.

The Z-Test (Known Population Variance)

A Z-test is used to determine whether a sample mean is significantly different from a population mean. It requires two strict conditions:

  1. The population standard deviation ($\sigma$) is known.
  2. The sample size is large (typically $n \ge 30$).

The Test Statistic follows the standard normal distribution under the null hypothesis:

$$z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$

Where:

  • $\bar{x}$ = sample mean
  • $\mu$ = population mean (under $H_0$)
  • $\sigma$ = population standard deviation
  • $n$ = sample size

A Common Trap: The Standard Error vs Standard Deviation

It is very easy to look at the formula denominator ($\sigma / \sqrt{n}$) and confuse it with the population standard deviation ($\sigma$). Let's break down exactly why we divide by $\sqrt{n}$.

Variance of the Mean

Each individual observation has a standard deviation of $\sigma$. But we aren't testing an individual; we are testing the sample mean ($\bar{x}$).

By the laws of variance for independent variables:

$$\mathrm{Var}\left(\frac{x_1+\cdots+x_n}{n}\right) = \frac{n\sigma^2}{n^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

Taking the square root gives us the standard deviation of the sample mean, also known as the Standard Error (SE):

$$\mathrm{SD}(\bar{x}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

The takeaway: When a problem states "Population standard deviation = $60$", they mean $\sigma = 60$, not $\sigma/\sqrt{n} = 60$. The spread of your sample mean shrinks as your sample size $n$ grows.

Worked Example: LED Bulbs

A manufacturer claims that the average lifetime of its LED bulbs is 1000 hours. A consumer agency randomly tests 50 bulbs and finds:

  • Sample mean lifetime = 980 hours
  • Population standard deviation = 60 hours

At a 5% significance level, test whether the manufacturer’s claim is valid.

Step 1: Set up hypotheses

$H_0: \mu = 1000$ hours
$H_1: \mu \neq 1000$ hours (two-tailed test)

Step 2: Compute the Z-statistic

$$Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{980 - 1000}{60/\sqrt{50}}$$

The denominator evaluates to $60/\sqrt{50} \approx 8.49$.

$$Z \approx \frac{-20}{8.49} \approx -2.36$$

Step 3: Decision rule

For a 5% significance, two-tailed test, our critical values are $Z = \pm 1.96$.
Since $|Z| = 2.36 > 1.96$, we fall into the rejection region.

Conclusion: Reject $H_0$. The probability of the mean deviating this much purely by chance is less than 5%.

The T-Test Family (Unknown Population Variance)

In the real world, we rarely know the true population standard deviation ($\sigma$). Instead, we estimate it using our sample standard deviation ($s$). Because we are estimating an extra parameter, we lose some certainty. This is why we use the t-distribution, which has "fatter tails" than the normal distribution to account for this extra uncertainty.

1. One-Sample T-Test

Used to compare a sample mean to a hypothesized population mean.

$$t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}$$

Degrees of Freedom (df): $n - 1$

Worked Example: Student Study Hours

A university claims that students study on average 6 hours per day. You randomly sample 10 students and observe:

  • Sample mean: $\bar{x} = 5.4$ hours
  • Sample standard deviation: $s = 0.8$ hours

Step 1: Hypotheses

$H_0: \mu = 6$
$H_1: \mu \neq 6$

Step 2: Compute the t-statistic

$$t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{s/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{5.4 - 6}{0.8/\sqrt{10}} \approx -2.37$$

Degrees of freedom: $df = n - 1 = 9$

Step 3: Decision ($\alpha = 0.05$)

The critical value for a two-tailed test with $df=9$ is $t_{0.025,9} \approx \pm 2.262$.
Since $|2.37| > 2.262$, the observed deviation is more extreme than what we allow under $\alpha = 0.05$. Reject $H_0$.

2. Paired T-Test

Use this when the same subjects are measured twice (e.g., a Before-After study).

The Key Insight

A paired t-test is essentially just a One-Sample T-test in disguise. We completely ignore the raw scores and instead calculate the differences ($d_i$) between the pairs. We then run a one-sample test on those differences against a hypothesized mean of 0.

$$t=\frac{\bar{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}$$

Where $\bar{d}$ is the mean of the differences, and $s_d$ is the standard deviation of those differences. $df = n - 1$.

Worked Example: Before-After Study

A trainer tests 8 students before and after a new study method:

StudentBeforeAfterDifference ($d$)
16065+5

37074+4

66467+3
75963+4

Step 1: Reduce to ONE sample

Our dataset is now simply: $d = [5, 3, 4, 4, 4, 3, 4, 4]$

Step 2: Compute Statistics

  • Mean difference: $\bar{d} = 3.875$
  • Sample standard deviation: $s_d \approx 0.64$
  • $n = 8$

Step 3: Compute the t-statistic

$$t = \frac{\bar{d} - 0}{s_d / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{3.875}{0.64/\sqrt{8}} \approx 17.1$$

Step 4: Decision

With $df = 7$ and $\alpha = 0.05$ (two-tailed), critical $t \approx \pm 2.365$.
Since $17.1 \gg 2.365$, we overwhelmingly Reject $H_0$.

3. Two-Sample T-Test (Independent Samples)

Used to compare the means of two completely independent groups.

Scenario A: Equal Variances (Pooled t-test)

$$t=\frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}}} \quad \text{where} \quad s_p^2=\frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}$$

Scenario B: Unequal Variances (Welch’s t-test)

$$t=\frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}$$
Worked Example: Online vs Classroom Training

We want to know if training method affects test scores.

  • Group 1 (Online): $n_1 = 20, \bar{x}_1 = 78, s_1 = 6$
  • Group 2 (Classroom): $n_2 = 22, \bar{x}_2 = 74, s_2 = 7$

Hypotheses: $H_0:\ \mu_1 = \mu_2 \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1:\ \mu_1 \ne \mu_2$

Test statistic (Assuming unequal variances):

$$t = \frac{78 - 74} {\sqrt{\frac{36}{20} + \frac{49}{22}}} = \frac{4}{\sqrt{1.8 + 2.227}} = \frac{4}{\sqrt{4.027}} \approx 1.99$$

Decision:

Using Welch's formula for degrees of freedom yields $df \approx 39$. The critical value at $\alpha = 0.05$ is $t \approx 2.02$.
Because $|1.99| < 2.02$, we Fail to reject $H_0$. There is not enough evidence to prove a difference.

Chi-Squared Test of Independence

While T-tests and Z-tests handle continuous numerical data, the Chi-square ($\chi^2$) test handles categorical data. It tests whether two categorical variables are independent of one another.

$$\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$$

Where $O$ = Observed frequency and $E$ = Expected frequency under $H_0$.

Worked Example: Study Method vs Exam Result

Is study method independent of the final exam result? A teacher records results for 100 students:

MethodPassFailTotal
Online302050
Offline401050
Total7030100

Step 1: Expected Counts

If they are independent, the expected count in any cell is: $E = \frac{(\text{row total})(\text{column total})}{\text{grand total}}$.

  • Online/Pass: $(50 \times 70)/100 = 35$
  • Online/Fail: $(50 \times 30)/100 = 15$
  • Offline/Pass: $(50 \times 70)/100 = 35$
  • Offline/Fail: $(50 \times 30)/100 = 15$

Step 2: Compute $\chi^2$ Statistic

$$\chi^2 = \frac{(30-35)^2}{35} + \frac{(20-15)^2}{15} + \frac{(40-35)^2}{35} + \frac{(10-15)^2}{15}$$
$$\chi^2 = \frac{25}{35} + \frac{25}{15} + \frac{25}{35} + \frac{25}{15} \approx 0.714 + 1.667 + 0.714 + 1.667 = 4.76$$

Step 3: Decision

Degrees of freedom: $df = (\text{rows}-1)(\text{cols}-1) = (2-1)(2-1) = 1$.

For $\alpha = 0.05$, the critical value $\chi^2_{0.05,1} = 3.84$.
Since $4.76 > 3.84$, we Reject $H_0$. Study method and exam result are dependent.
