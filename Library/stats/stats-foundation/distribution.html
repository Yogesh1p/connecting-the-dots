If a distribution depends on parameters $\theta$, we write:

$$ X \sim \mathcal{D}(\theta) $$

This means $X$ follows the distribution $\mathcal{D}$ with parameter(s) $\theta$. The symbol " $\sim$ " is read as "is distributed as" or "follows".

1. $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$

(Bernoulli trial). An experiment that can result in either a "success" or a "failure" (but not both). A Bernoulli random variable can be thought of as the indicator of success: it equals 1 if success occurs and 0 if failure occurs. It is a discrete random variable that can take on only two possible values, 0 and 1.

PMF (Probability Mass Function) $$ P(X=x) = \begin{cases} p, & x=1 \\ 1-p, & x=0 \end{cases} $$ Compact: $P(X=x) = p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$
CDF (Cumulative Distribution) $$ F(x) = \begin{cases} 0, & x \lt 0 \\ 1-p, & 0 \le x \lt 1 \\ 1, & x \ge 1 \end{cases} $$
Mean & Variance $E[X] = p$
$\operatorname{Var}(X) = p(1-p)$
Median & Mode $\text{Mode/Median} = \begin{cases} 0, & p \lt \tfrac{1}{2} \\ \{0,1\}, & p = \tfrac{1}{2} \\ 1, & p \gt \tfrac{1}{2} \end{cases}$

Equivalencies: $\text{Bernoulli}(p) \equiv \text{Binomial}(n=1,p)$

2. $X \sim \text{Binomial}(n,p)$

Suppose that $n$ independent Bernoulli trials are performed, each with the same success probability $p$. Let $X$ be the number of successes. We write $X \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$, where $n$ is a positive integer and $0 < p < 1$.

PMF $$ P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k} $$ for $k=0,1,\dots,n$
CDF (No closed-form) $$ F(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor x\rfloor}\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k} $$
Mean & Variance $E[X] = np$
$\operatorname{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$
Median & Mode $m \in \{\lfloor np \rfloor, \lceil np \rceil\}$
$\lfloor (n+1)p \rfloor$ (if $(n+1)p \notin \mathbb{Z}$)

3. Uniform Distributions

Discrete Uniform ($X \sim \text{DUnif}(C)$): Choose one number uniformly at random from a finite, nonempty set $C$. All values are equally likely.

Continuous Uniform ($X \sim \text{Uniform}(a,b)$): A random variable $X$ has equal probability density over every point in the continuous interval $[a, b]$.

PDF (Continuous) $$ f(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{b-a}, & a \le x \le b \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$
CDF (Continuous) $$ F(x)= \begin{cases} 0, & x \lt a \\ \frac{x-a}{b-a}, & a \le x \le b \\ 1, & x \gt b \end{cases} $$
Mean & Variance $E[X] = \frac{a+b}{2}$
$\operatorname{Var}(X) = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$
Median & Mode $\frac{a+b}{2}$
Every point is equally likely (No unique mode).

4. $X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)$

A continuous distribution used for modeling waiting times between independent events that occur at a constant average rate $\lambda \gt 0$. It is uniquely characterized by its memoryless property.

PDF $$ f(x) = \begin{cases} \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, & x \ge 0 \\ 0, & x \lt 0 \end{cases} $$
CDF $$ F(x)= \begin{cases} 1 - e^{-\lambda x}, & x \ge 0 \\ 0, & x \lt 0 \end{cases} $$
Mean & Variance $E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda}$
$\operatorname{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}$
Median & Mode $\frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}$
$0$
Survival Function: $S(x)=P(X \gt x)= e^{-\lambda x}$

Memoryless Property: $P(X \gt s + t \mid X \gt t) = P(X \gt s)$

Relationship with Poisson Process: If events occur according to a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, the waiting time between events is $\text{Exponential}(\lambda)$.

5. $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$

A discrete distribution used to model the number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space, when events happen independently and with a constant average rate $\lambda \gt 0$.

PMF $$ P(X = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} $$ for $k = 0,1,2,\dots$
CDF $$ F(k) = \sum_{i=0}^{k} \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^i}{i!} $$
Mean & Variance $E[X] = \lambda$
$\operatorname{Var}(X) = \lambda$
Mode $\lfloor \lambda \rfloor$ (if $\lambda \notin \mathbb{Z}$)
  • Additivity: If $X_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ and $X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ are independent, $X_1 + X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$.
  • Approximation to Binomial: If $n$ is large, $p$ is small, and $\lambda = np$, then $\text{Binomial}(n,p) \approx \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$. Useful for rare-event modeling.

6 & 7. The Normal Distributions

A continuous, symmetric, bell-shaped distribution widely used due to the Central Limit Theorem. $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ where $\mu$ is the mean and $\sigma^2$ is the variance.

Standard Normal: $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ If $Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$ then $Z$ is standard normal. $$ \phi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2} $$
General Normal PDF $$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left( -\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) $$
68–95–99.7 Rule (Empirical Rule):
68% of observations lie in $\mu \pm \sigma$
95% lie in $\mu \pm 2\sigma$
99.7% lie in $\mu \pm 3\sigma$

8. T-distribution & 9. Chi-squared

T-distribution: Used instead of normal when the sample size is small and variance is unknown. It has heavier tails to account for extra uncertainty.

Chi-squared distribution: Models the variability of squared normal deviations. Used to study variance, goodness of fit, and independence.

Geometric & First Success Distributions

Geometric ($X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$): The number of failures before the first successful trial. PMF is $P(X=k) = q^k p$ for $k=0,1,2,\dots$, where $q = 1-p$.

First Success ($Y \sim \text{FS}(p)$): The number of trials until the first successful trial, including the success. If $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$, then $X + 1 \sim \text{FS}(p)$.
