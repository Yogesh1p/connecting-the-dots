Part I: Probability

1. Sample Space and Event

The sample space $\Omega$ (or $S$) of an experiment is the set of all possible outcomes of the experiment. An event $A$ is a subset of the sample space $\Omega$, and we say that $A$ occurred if the actual outcome is in $A$.

2. Naive Definition of Probability

Let $A$ be an event for an experiment with a finite sample space $S$. The naive probability of $A$ is defined as:

$$ P_{\text{naive}}(A) = \frac{|A|}{|S|} = \frac{\text{number of outcomes favorable to } A}{\text{total number of outcomes in } S} $$

3. General Definition & Kolmogorov’s Axioms

A probability space consists of a sample space $\Omega$ and a probability function $P$ that takes an event $A \subseteq \Omega$ as input and returns a real number $P(A) \in [0,1]$. The probability axioms are the foundational rules that any probability measure must satisfy:

Axiom 1: Non-negativity $$ P(A) \ge 0 $$

For every event $A$.

Axiom 2: Normalization $$ P(\Omega) = 1 $$

The probability that something in the sample space happens is 1.

Axiom 3: Countable Additivity ($\sigma$-additivity)

For any countable collection of mutually exclusive (disjoint) events $A_1, A_2, \dots$ (meaning $A_i \cap A_j = \varnothing$ for $i \neq j$):

$$ P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i) $$

From just these three axioms, we can derive the following essential properties:

  • Probability of the empty set: $P(\varnothing) = 0$
  • Bounds: $0 \le P(A) \le 1$
  • Complement: $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$
  • Inclusion-Exclusion: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$
  • Monotonicity: $A \subseteq B \implies P(A) \le P(B)$

4. De Morgan’s Laws (for events)

For any two events $A$ and $B$:

$$ (A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c $$ $$ (A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c $$

5. Conditional Probability & Independence

The probability of event $A$ occurring given that event $B$ has already occurred is defined as:

$$ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad \text{provided } P(B) \gt 0 $$

Two events $A$ and $B$ are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other. Mathematically, they are independent if and only if:

$$ P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B) \quad \iff \quad P(A \mid B) = P(A) $$

6. Law of Total Probability & Bayes' Theorem

If $B_1, B_2, \dots, B_n$ form a partition of the sample space (they are disjoint and their union is $\Omega$), then for any event $A$:

$$ P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A \mid B_i)P(B_i) $$

Bayes' Theorem allows us to update probabilities based on new evidence. It relates $P(A \mid B)$ to $P(B \mid A)$:

$$ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)P(A)}{P(B)} $$

7. Inclusion–Exclusion

For any events \(A_1, A_2, \dots, A_n\),

$$ \begin{aligned} P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right) &= \sum_i P(A_i) - \sum_{i \lt j} P(A_i \cap A_j) + \sum_{i \lt j \lt k} P(A_i \cap A_j \cap A_k) - \cdots \\ &\quad + (-1)^{n+1} P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) \end{aligned} $$

This formula follows the same intuition as the two-event case: first add all individual probabilities, then subtract the pairwise overlaps that were counted twice, then add back the triple overlaps that were subtracted too many times, and continue alternating.

Part II: Counting

1. Multiplication Rule

Consider a compound experiment consisting of two sub-experiments, Experiment A and Experiment B. Suppose that Experiment A has $a$ possible outcomes, and for each of those outcomes Experiment B has $b$ possible outcomes. Then the compound experiment has $ab$ possible outcomes.

2. Subsets

A set with $n$ elements has $2^n$ subsets. This is because for each element, there are 2 choices: either include it in the subset or exclude it.

3. Sampling With Replacement (Independent Choices)

Imagine drawing from a pool of $n$ distinct objects $k$ times. If each drawn object is returned to the pool before the next draw, every choice is a fresh start. Because order matters and the pool size never shrinks, the total number of possible outcomes scales exponentially:

$$ n^k $$

4. Permutations (Sampling Without Replacement)

What happens if we remove the replacement rule? When drawing $k$ objects from a pool of $n$, each choice permanently removes an option from the pool. Your first choice has $n$ possibilities, your second has $n-1$, and so on.

When order matters, this sequence of shrinking choices is called a permutation (${}^nP_k$):

$$ n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-k+1) $$

More elegantly, we can represent this by taking the factorial of the entire set and dividing out the unselected remainder. This yields outcomes for $1 \le k \le n$ (and 0 possibilities if $k \gt n$):

$$ {}^nP_k = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} $$

(Note: A factorial, written as $n!$, simply represents the arrangement of all $n$ items, mathematically defined as $n(n-1)\cdots 1$, with $0! = 1$ by convention.)

5. Combinations (Unordered Subsets)

Often, we only care about which items were selected, not the sequence in which they were drawn. To find the number of unique subsets of size $k$ from $n$ items, we start with the permutations calculated above and divide out the redundant arrangements.

Since $k$ selected items can be arranged in $k!$ different ways, we divide our permutation formula by $k!$. This gives us the binomial coefficient, read as "$n$ choose $k$":

$${}^nC_k =\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} $$

6. The Binomial Theorem

We can apply this combinatorial intuition directly to algebra. When expanding $(x+y)^n$, you are multiplying $(x+y)$ by itself $n$ times. To form each term in the final expansion, you must choose either an $x$ or a $y$ from each of the $n$ factors.

If you choose $y$ exactly $k$ times, you are forced to choose $x$ the remaining $n-k$ times, resulting in the term $x^{n-k}y^k$. How many different ways can you select $k$ instances of $y$ from $n$ factors? Exactly $\binom{n}{k}$ ways. Summing all possible values of $k$ gives us the Binomial Theorem:

$$ (x+y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n}\binom{n}{k}x^{n-k}y^k $$

7. Multinomial Coefficient

If we want to divide $n$ distinct items into $r$ distinct groups of sizes $k_1, k_2, \dots, k_r$ (where $\sum k_i = n$), the number of ways to do this is given by the multinomial coefficient:

$$ \binom{n}{k_1, k_2, \dots, k_r} = \frac{n!}{k_1! k_2! \cdots k_r!} $$

8. Stars and Bars (Bose-Einstein)

Definition (Divider Method): The number of ways to distribute $n$ indistinguishable balls into $k$ distinguishable bins is:

$$ \binom{n + k - 1}{k - 1} \;=\; \binom{n + k - 1}{n} $$

This is because we represent the $n$ balls by $n$ stars and separate the $k$ bins using $k-1$ bars.

Example:

How many ways can we assign 20 students to 4 different professors, assuming the students are indistinguishable and each professor only cares about how many students they receive?

Solution:

This is a stars and bars problem with 20 stars and $4-1=3$ bars. Hence, the number of ways is:

$$ \binom{20 + 4 - 1}{4 - 1} = \binom{23}{3} = \binom{23}{20} $$

9. Vandermonde’s Identity

Choosing $k$ items from a total of $m+n$ (divided into two groups of $m$ and $n$) is equivalent to picking $j$ items from the first group and $k-j$ from the second, summed over all possible $j$.

$$ \binom{m+n}{k} = \sum_{j=0}^{k} \binom{m}{j}\binom{n}{k-j} $$
