Language Is Not Thought

Large Language Models transformed artificial intelligence through a remarkably simple objective: predict the next token. Given enough data and scale, this produced systems capable of translation, coding, reasoning, and conversation. To many observers, this success suggested that intelligence itself might simply be sophisticated language prediction.

But there is a problem with that idea. Human cognition does not appear to operate primarily through language.

Consider cutting vegetables. Before reaching for a knife, most people do not construct a verbal plan. They imagine the situation — the cutting board, the knife, the motion. They mentally simulate the sequence of actions. Language arrives only if they need to explain the process to someone else.

Writing follows a similar pattern. An article often begins as a collection of vague intuitions, half-formed relationships, and images. Converting those into sentences is a separate step that happens afterward. The thought precedes the sentence.

Language may not be thought itself. It may simply be the interface through which thought is communicated.

If this is true, modeling intelligence as next-token prediction is a category error. Intelligence may require constructing, simulating, and reasoning over internal representations of the world — not merely predicting the surface of language.

The Odd Middle Ground of Language Models

Here is where things get interesting. Modern language models are not as far from this picture as they first appear.

Internally, transformers do not reason directly over words. Every token is first mapped into a high-dimensional vector. The network performs its computation in that vector space before projecting the result back into a probability distribution over vocabulary. The words are only the input and output; the actual processing happens in a continuous latent space.

The clearest evidence comes from multilingual models. Ask a model "What is gravity?", "¿Qué es la gravedad?", and "重力是什么?" and it produces nearly identical explanations. The model is not storing separate reasoning procedures for every language. Different languages appear to map into a shared conceptual space before being decoded back into text. Translation capabilities emerge without ever building a translation system.

In other words, modern language models already contain something that resembles a language-independent semantic space. They do not literally think in English. They map language into latent representations, perform computation in that space, and decode the result back into text. Researchers sometimes say that LLMs know more than their architecture lets them express.

Perhaps intelligence primarily operates in a continuous latent space, while language is merely a lossy projection of that space used for communication.

This raises a genuinely puzzling question. If language models already develop something like a thought space, what exactly is missing?

The Missing Workspace

Return to the vegetable example. The mental simulation you ran before reaching for the knife was not merely verbal. You modelled objects, locations, actions, and their consequences — something closer to:

Current state → Reach for knife → Knife acquired → Move to board → Vegetables cut

This is planning inside a latent state space, not language generation. And crucially, it is revisable. If you imagine picking up the knife and realize the vegetables are not yet washed, you update the plan. You do not start over.

Autoregressive language models work differently. Their training objective is fundamentally sequential: generate one token, then the next, always moving forward. While external scaffolding — memory systems, tool calls, iterative prompting — can layer revision on top, the core model is not trained to maintain an editable internal workspace. It is trained to continue a sequence.

There is no latent canvas where intermediate states can be held, evaluated, and modified. To revise an image it generated, an LLM must generate it, reload it as input, and perform a separate editing pass. A human sketching a car, on the other hand, simply picks up the pencil and adds the missing logos.

This is the key gap. Not intelligence, and not the latent space — which LLMs already possess — but the ability to reason, simulate, and plan inside that space before committing to an output.

This gap points toward an old idea with renewed relevance: the world model.

Architecture — Predictive Representations

The Critical Distinction: What Is Being Predicted?

Accepting that LLMs have a latent space is not enough. The crucial question is what the training objective does with that space. And here, the difference between current LLMs and world model architectures becomes precise.

In a language model, the latent space is a means to an end. It is learned because it helps predict the next token. The loss function is applied over vocabulary probabilities, not over representations themselves. The internal space is free to organize however it likes, as long as the token predictions come out right.

In a world model, the latent representation is the end itself. The system is explicitly trained to predict future states in latent space — to model what the world will look like after an action, not what word comes next in a sentence. This difference in training objective is what separates the two approaches.

An analogy: imagine teaching a child physics. One method is to have them read physics textbooks and predict the next word on each page. Eventually, through sheer exposure, they might infer the underlying laws. Another method is to have them watch objects move and predict what those objects will do next — training physical intuition directly. LeCun's argument is that the second method is more fundamental, and that current AI has been doing the first.

Making It Concrete: How an LLM Actually Computes

The distinction is easy to state abstractly. It becomes much clearer when you trace what actually happens inside a language model step by step.

Suppose the input is: 

The cat sat on the ...

The transformer processes this sequence and produces a hidden vector h. This is the latent representation — a point in a high-dimensional space that encodes everything the model has inferred about the context. So far, so good.

Then the model applies a projection:

logits = h · W

Where W is a matrix of vocabulary embeddings. The result is a score for every word in the vocabulary. A softmax converts those scores into probabilities. The model is trained so that the highest-probability token here is "mat."

Notice what the loss function sees: it checks only whether the right token won. It does not care what h looks like internally. The latent space is free to organize itself in any way that helps produce correct token predictions. It is an instrument, not a target.

"Aren't These Basically the Same Thing?"

Here is the confusion that trips almost everyone up the first time they hear about JEPA:

"An LLM predicts a hidden representation and then converts it to a token. JEPA predicts a hidden representation. Aren't these basically the same thing?"

Architecturally: somewhat. Training-wise: no. The difference is where the loss is applied.

In a language model, the loss lands on the token. The representation is upstream of the loss — it is a stepping stone toward the thing being measured. In JEPA, the loss lands directly on the representation. The gap being minimized is:

LLM loss

predicted_token  vs  actual_token
JEPA loss

predicted_representation  vs  actual_representation

In the JEPA case, the model is not asked "did you predict 'mat'?" — it is asked "did you predict the right state of the world?" No words are involved. The target is: what is the underlying latent state that the next observation would encode?

A Strange Implication for AI Safety

This architecture reveals something counterintuitive about how LLMs handle meaning — and it has an unexpected connection to AI safety research.

Models are often trained not to produce certain categories of content. Yet users sometimes bypass these restrictions by rephrasing requests using synonyms or indirect language. At first glance, this seems to suggest the model is responding to surface linguistic patterns — specific words — rather than the underlying concepts those words name.

But that conclusion conflicts with what we know about the internal geometry. Words like sex, intercourse, and fornication are represented by vectors that sit close together in the model's latent space. The model clearly knows these concepts are related. So why does a surface-level synonym sometimes change its behavior?

The answer connects directly to the token-prediction objective. Because safety training is applied to outputs — to the tokens the model generates — the boundaries it learns are not clean conceptual walls. They are imperfect carved regions inside a messy, continuous semantic space, shaped by which linguistic framings happened to appear in training. The model knows the concept but has learned to respond differently depending on how the concept is approached through language.

A system trained to predict representations rather than tokens might behave differently here — its safety mechanisms could operate directly on the concept rather than on the linguistic surface. Whether that would make alignment easier or harder is an open research question.

The Pixel Trap and the Blur of Uncertainty

To understand why predicting representations rather than tokens matters so much, consider what happened when researchers tried to extend the LLM paradigm to video.

Language has a fixed, discrete vocabulary. If a model is unsure whether a sentence ends with "cat" or "dog," it assigns probabilities to each and the training objective handles the ambiguity cleanly. But pixel prediction has no such vocabulary. When researchers trained models to predict the exact pixel values of the next video frame, the results were disastrous.

Imagine training a neural network on a video of a bouncing ball. At the top of its arc, the ball could fall slightly to the left, or slightly to the right. The generative video model is forced to output a single frame of pixels. Confronted with this physical ambiguity, the math of the neural network does the only thing it can: it hedges its bets. It averages the pixels of the "left bounce" and the "right bounce" together. The result is a blurry, washed-out smear. As you try to predict further into the future, the blur compounds into nothingness.

"If you train a generative model to predict what happens in a dashcam video, it will spend most of its resources predicting the random motion of the leaves on the trees... things that are essentially unpredictable, but have a lot of pixels."
— Yann LeCun

This is the Pixel Trap. Reconstructive AI forces models to care about the unpredictable, irrelevant noise of reality (the leaves) instead of the underlying physics (the road).

Escaping the Canvas: Joint Embeddings

If generating raw pixels is a fool's errand, we must throw away the canvas. We do not need a model to paint reality; we need a model to understand it.

This insight led to the resurgence of Joint Embedding Architectures (tracing back to Siamese networks in the 1990s). The idea is elegant: instead of taking an image $X$ and trying to generate a corrupted version of it $Y$, we pass both $X$ and $Y$ through identical encoder networks. We then instruct the network: Make the output mathematical vectors (embeddings) of these two images identical.

$$E(X) \approx E(Y)$$

By forcing the embeddings to match, the encoder is forced to ignore the surface-level noise (the blur, the color shift) and extract the semantic "essence" of the image.

The Villain: Representation Collapse

Joint embeddings sound perfect, but they harbor a fatal flaw. If the only objective is to make $E(X)$ and $E(Y)$ similar, the neural network will quickly discover a trivial cheat code. It will learn to output a vector of all ones—e.g., $[1, 1, 1, 1]$—for every single image it ever sees.

If the embedding for a dog is $[1, 1]$ and the embedding for a corrupted dog is $[1, 1]$, the loss is zero. The network has solved the math problem, but it has learned absolutely nothing about the world. This catastrophic failure mode is known as Representation Collapse.

For years, the only solution was Contrastive Learning: feeding the network negative examples (e.g., a picture of a cat) and explicitly forcing the embeddings apart. But contrastive learning is computationally brutal, requiring massive batch sizes to find meaningful "negatives."

The Barlow Twins Epiphany

In 2021, LeCun's team found a mathematical bypass to collapse based on a 1961 theory by neuroscientist Horace Barlow: sensory pathways operate by reducing redundancy between neurons.

Instead of comparing different images, the Barlow Twins architecture computes a cross-correlation matrix $\mathcal{C}$ between the outputs of the individual neurons across the two encoders. The loss function then forces this matrix to look exactly like an Identity Matrix:

$$ \mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\sum_i (1 - \mathcal{C}_{ii})^2}_{\text{Invariance Term}} + \lambda \underbrace{\sum_i \sum_{j \neq i} \mathcal{C}_{ij}^2}_{\text{Redundancy Reduction Term}} $$
  • The Diagonal ($\mathcal{C}_{ii} \rightarrow 1$): Forces the same neuron in both networks to fire identically for the same concept, ensuring the model is invariant to noise.
  • The Off-Diagonal ($\mathcal{C}_{ij} \rightarrow 0$): Forces different neurons to decorrelate. If Neuron 1 learns "fur," Neuron 2 cannot also learn "fur." This prevents all neurons from collapsing into a single, lazy pattern.

JEPA: The Architecture of World Models

With representation collapse solved without the need for negative examples (via Barlow Twins, VICReg, and later DINO), the stage was set for the architecture LeCun had been working toward: JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture).

JEPA asks: If we have a robust latent space, can we do physics in it? Instead of predicting the embedding of the same image, JEPA attempts to predict the embedding of the future state of the world, conditioned on an action.

Given the current state of a robot arm (Sₜ) and the action "move left" (Aₜ), the predictor outputs the embedding of the arm after moving — not a rendered image of it. The model learns to reason about the consequences of actions inside latent space, which is exactly the editable mental workspace that standard LLMs lack.

Where the Debate Stands

This is where a genuine scientific disagreement lives.

LeCun's position is roughly: next-token prediction is the wrong objective. It forces models to expend capacity on irrelevant surface details — the leaves on the trees, the exact wording of a sentence — rather than building models of the underlying world. JEPA-style training makes the right thing the thing being optimized.

The opposing view is equally coherent: the latent space already emerges inside large language models. The multilingual evidence, the analogical reasoning, the emergent planning — all suggest that next-token prediction, applied at scale, forces the network to develop increasingly sophisticated internal representations of reality anyway. Maybe the objective does not matter as much as the scale.

LLM: the latent space is learned because it helps predict tokens. JEPA: the latent space is the thing being predicted directly. In one, the representation is a means to an end. In the other, it is the end itself.

Recent research directions suggest the field is hedging toward the JEPA view without abandoning language. World models, latent reasoning, diffusion language models, planning agents, and multimodal architectures all share a common thread: build a richer internal representation of reality, and use language as one output interface among many — not as the medium of thought itself.

Perhaps the most honest summary is this. Today's LLMs are in an odd middle ground: internally, they appear to develop genuine semantic concepts; externally, they are trained as token predictors. JEPA is a proposal to make the internal reality the explicit object of training. Whether that requires a fundamentally new architecture or simply different objectives applied to existing ones is the question modern AI research is in the process of answering.

What seems clear is the destination. Intelligence — whether in humans or machines — appears to operate primarily inside a continuous latent space, constructing models of the world, simulating possible futures, and editing plans before committing to action. Language, in this picture, is not the medium of thought. It is the interface through which a deeper process communicates with the world.
