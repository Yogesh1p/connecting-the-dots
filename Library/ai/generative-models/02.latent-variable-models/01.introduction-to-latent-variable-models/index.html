Why Do We Need Latent Variables?

Imagine you need to draw a perfect circle on a blank piece of paper. If you try to draw it dot by dot, starting from the top edge and guessing where the next tiny mark should go, it is incredibly difficult. The very first dot you draw has no real meaning on its own.

This is the main limitation of purely autoregressive models. They generate data one small piece at a time, relying entirely on the sequence of what they have drawn so far. They do not start with a "big picture" plan. Notice in the animation below how the autoregressive model must build the shape by connecting local pieces, constantly looking back at its history to figure out the next step.

The Compass Analogy

How do humans actually draw a circle? We do not guess dot by dot. We first decide two global properties: the center and the radius.

Once these high-level rules are decided, we place a drawing compass at the center, set the width, and trace the line. The location of the very first mark on the paper is not random—it is completely controlled by our prior, hidden choice of center and radius.

In machine learning, these hidden, high-level decisions are called latent variables (usually written as $Z$). They hold the abstract blueprint of the data, such as its shape, position, or style. The final data we actually see ($X$) is then generated from this blueprint. We write this cause-and-effect relationship simply as:

$$Z \rightarrow X$$
