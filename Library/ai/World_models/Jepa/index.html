Architecture — Implicit Likelihood Models

The Pixel Trap and the Blur of Uncertainty

Large Language Models (LLMs) conquered the world by playing a very simple game: predict the next token. Because language has a fixed, discrete vocabulary, if an LLM is unsure whether a sentence ends with "cat" or "dog," it simply assigns a 40% probability to one and 60% to the other.

When researchers tried to apply this exact same self-supervised, generative approach to video—training a model to predict the exact pixel values of the next frame—the results were disastrous.

Imagine training a neural network on a video of a bouncing ball. At the top of its arc, the ball could fall slightly to the left, or slightly to the right. The generative video model is forced to output a single frame of pixels. Confronted with this physical ambiguity, the math of the neural network does the only thing it can: it hedges its bets. It averages the pixels of the "left bounce" and the "right bounce" together. The result is a blurry, washed-out smear. As you try to predict further into the future, the blur compounds into nothingness.

"If you train a generative model to predict what happens in a dashcam video, it will spend most of its resources predicting the random motion of the leaves on the trees... things that are essentially unpredictable, but have a lot of pixels."
— Yann LeCun

This is the Pixel Trap. Reconstructive AI forces models to care about the unpredictable, irrelevant noise of reality (the leaves) instead of the underlying physics (the road).

Escaping the Canvas: Joint Embeddings

If generating raw pixels is a fool's errand, we must throw away the canvas. We do not need a model to paint reality; we need a model to understand it.

This insight led to the resurgence of Joint Embedding Architectures (tracing back to Siamese networks in the 1990s). The idea is elegant: instead of taking an image $X$ and trying to generate a corrupted version of it $Y$, we pass both $X$ and $Y$ through identical encoder networks. We then instruct the network: Make the output mathematical vectors (embeddings) of these two images identical.

$$E(X) \approx E(Y)$$

By forcing the embeddings to match, the encoder is forced to ignore the surface-level noise (the blur, the color shift) and extract the semantic "essence" of the image.

The Villain: Representation Collapse

Joint embeddings sound perfect, but they harbor a fatal flaw. If the only objective is to make $E(X)$ and $E(Y)$ similar, the neural network will quickly discover a trivial cheat code. It will learn to output a vector of all ones—e.g., $[1, 1, 1, 1]$—for every single image it ever sees.

If the embedding for a dog is $[1, 1]$ and the embedding for a corrupted dog is $[1, 1]$, the loss is zero. The network has solved the math problem, but it has learned absolutely nothing about the world. This catastrophic failure mode is known as Representation Collapse.

For years, the only solution was Contrastive Learning: feeding the network negative examples (e.g., a picture of a cat) and explicitly forcing the embeddings apart. But contrastive learning is computationally brutal, requiring massive batch sizes to find meaningful "negatives."

The Barlow Twins Epiphany

In 2021, LeCun's team found a mathematical bypass to collapse based on a 1961 theory by neuroscientist Horace Barlow: sensory pathways operate by reducing redundancy between neurons.

Instead of comparing different images, the Barlow Twins architecture computes a cross-correlation matrix $\mathcal{C}$ between the outputs of the individual neurons across the two encoders. The loss function then forces this matrix to look exactly like an Identity Matrix:

$$ \mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\sum_i (1 - \mathcal{C}_{ii})^2}_{\text{Invariance Term}} + \lambda \underbrace{\sum_i \sum_{j \neq i} \mathcal{C}_{ij}^2}_{\text{Redundancy Reduction Term}} $$
  • The Diagonal ($\mathcal{C}_{ii} \rightarrow 1$): Forces the same neuron in both networks to fire identically for the same concept, ensuring the model is invariant to noise.
  • The Off-Diagonal ($\mathcal{C}_{ij} \rightarrow 0$): Forces different neurons to decorrelate. If Neuron 1 learns "fur," Neuron 2 cannot also learn "fur." This prevents all neurons from collapsing into a single, lazy pattern.

JEPA: The Architecture of World Models

With representation collapse solved without the need for negative examples (via Barlow Twins, VICReg, and later DINO), the stage was set for the ultimate architecture: JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture).

JEPA asks: If we have a robust latent space, can we do physics in it? Instead of predicting the embedding of the same image, JEPA attempts to predict the embedding of the future state of the world, often conditioned on an action.

If you give the Predictor the current state of a robot arm ($S_t$) and tell it the action "move left" ($A_t$), it outputs the embedding of the arm moved to the left ($S_{t+1}$).

Because inference is now a search process inside a highly compressed, physics-aware latent space, the model can simulate hundreds of possible futures in milliseconds. It doesn't blindly output an action; it plans. It is a true World Model. Airlines do not have world models—they cannot foresee a storm before they fly into it. Autonomous agents must.
