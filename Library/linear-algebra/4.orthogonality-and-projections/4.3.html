The Projection Matrix: Extreme Cases

Recall our master formula for projecting any vector $b$ onto the column space of a matrix $A$. The projection matrix $P$ physically forces any vector into the nearest point within that allowed subspace:

$$P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$$

To build an intuition for what this mathematical machinery is doing, it helps to test the boundaries. What happens at the extremes?

  1. Case 1: $b$ is already in the column space.
    If $b$ lives in the column space, it must be a linear combination of the columns of $A$. Mathematically, this means $b = Ax$ for some vector $x$. If we push this through our projection formula, we get:
    $$Pb = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T (Ax)$$
    Notice that $A^T A$ is sitting right next to its own inverse, $(A^T A)^{-1}$. They obliterate each other, leaving just $Ax$. And since $Ax = b$, the projection of $b$ is simply $b$. The matrix recognized the vector was already home and left it untouched.
  2. Case 2: $b$ is perfectly perpendicular to the column space.
    If $b$ is orthogonal to the column space $C(A)$, it must live entirely in the orthogonal complement. From the fundamental theorem of linear algebra, we know the orthogonal complement of the column space is the null space of $A^T$. Therefore, $A^T b = 0$.

    Look at the far right side of the projection formula: $Pb = A(A^T A)^{-1} (A^T b)$. Because $A^T b = 0$, the entire expression collapses. $Pb = 0$. The projection of a purely orthogonal vector onto the subspace is the zero vector. It casts no shadow.

Fitting a Straight Line: The Intuition of Least Squares

When do we actually need this? The most vital application in science, statistics, and machine learning is finding order in messy data. Suppose we have three measurements taken at times $t = 1, 2, 3$. The observed values are $b = 1, 2, 2$.

We want to fit these points to a straight line model: $C + Dt = b$. Let's plug our data into the model to build our system of equations:

$$C + 1D = 1$$ $$C + 2D = 2$$ $$C + 3D = 2$$

If you plot these three points, you will immediately see they form a triangle, not a straight line. No line can pass through all three points simultaneously. In matrix form, $Ax = b$ looks like this:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} C \\ D \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Our vector $b = (1, 2, 2)$ is not in the column space of $A$. The system is unsolvable. But we cannot just give up. We need the best possible solution. We define the "best" line as the one that minimizes the sum of the squared errors between the line and the actual data points. We want to minimize $\|Ax - b\|^2$.

There are two distinct ways to conceptualize this minimization: calculus and geometry.

The Calculus Perspective

We want to minimize the total squared error, which is the sum of the squares of the vertical misses at each point:

$$E = (C + D - 1)^2 + (C + 2D - 2)^2 + (C + 3D - 2)^2$$

To find the minimum of a multivariable function, we take the partial derivatives with respect to $C$ and $D$, and set them both to zero. Because the error function is a sum of squares, the derivatives will be perfectly linear. This produces two equations with two unknowns.

The Linear Algebra Perspective

Instead of taking derivatives, we rely on the geometry of projection. We want the vector $p$ in the column space of $A$ that is closest to $b$. The error vector $e = b - p$ must be completely orthogonal to the column space. As we proved previously, this requirement directly yields the Normal Equation:

The Normal Equation
$$A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$$

There is another way to view this step. The original system $Ax = b$ may fail because $b$ does not lie in the column space of $A$. By multiplying both sides by $A^T$, we transform the equation into $A^T A x = A^T b$, where both sides now lie in the row space of $A$. Since $A^T b$ always belongs to this space, the new system is guaranteed to be consistent. In this sense, the Normal Equation shifts the problem into a space where it can always be solved.

If you actually take the partial derivatives from the calculus approach, set them to zero, and rearrange the terms, you will derive this exact same matrix equation. The spatial intuition of dropping a perpendicular shadow and the analytic intuition of finding a derivative minimum converge on the exact same truth.

Solving the System

Let's calculate the components of our Normal Equation to find our best coefficients $\hat{x}$. First, compute $A^T A$:

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{bmatrix}$$

Notice that $A^T A$ is beautifully symmetric. Next, compute $A^T b$:

$$A^T b = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix}$$

Now we solve $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} C \\ D \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix}$$

Subtracting twice the first row from the second row gives $2D = 1 \implies D = 1/2$. Back-substituting into the first row gives $3C + 3 = 5 \implies C = 2/3$.

Our best fit line is $y = \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{2}t$.

Validating the Error

Let's project our points onto this new line to find the vector $p$, which contains the predicted heights:

  • At $t=1$: $p_1 = 2/3 + 1/2(1) = 7/6$
  • At $t=2$: $p_2 = 2/3 + 1/2(2) = 5/3 = 10/6$
  • At $t=3$: $p_3 = 2/3 + 1/2(3) = 13/6$

The error vector is $e = b - p$.

$$e = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 7/6 \\ 10/6 \\ 13/6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1/6 \\ 2/6 \\ -1/6 \end{bmatrix}$$

If our math is correct, this error vector must be orthogonal to the entire column space of $A$. Let's test it against the two basis columns of $A$:

$e \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = -1/6 + 2/6 - 1/6 = 0$

$e \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = -1/6 + 4/6 - 3/6 = 0$

The dot products are zero. The error is perfectly perpendicular to the plane spanned by our model.

Why $A^T A$ is Always Invertible (If Columns are Independent)

We rely heavily on solving $(A^T A)^{-1}$. But how do we know $A^T A$ is invertible? The matrix $A^T A$ is guaranteed to be invertible as long as the columns of $A$ are linearly independent. Let's prove it directly.

Assume there is a vector $x$ in the null space of $A^T A$. This means:

$$A^T A x = 0$$

Multiply both sides by $x^T$:

$$x^T A^T A x = 0$$

Using transpose properties, $(Ax)^T = x^T A^T$, so we can rewrite this as:

$$(Ax)^T (Ax) = 0$$

The dot product of a vector with itself is its squared length: $\|Ax\|^2 = 0$. The only vector with a length of zero is the zero vector itself, so $Ax = 0$.

Now we use our condition: if $A$ has independent columns, the only solution to $Ax = 0$ is $x = 0$. Therefore, the null space of $A^T A$ contains only the zero vector. A matrix with an empty null space is strictly invertible.

Looking Ahead: Orthonormal Vectors

There is a specific scenario where the math of $A^T A$ becomes exceptionally beautiful: when the columns of $A$ are independent and perpendicular unit vectors. We call these orthonormal vectors.

If the columns of $A$ are orthonormal, their dot products with themselves are 1, and their dot products with each other are 0. In this magical case, $A^T A$ collapses completely into the Identity Matrix ($I$).
