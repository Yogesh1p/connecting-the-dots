The Final Factorization: SVD

The Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) brings together everything we have studied about linear transformations, orthogonal matrices, and the four fundamental subspaces. Any matrix whatsoever can be broken down into:

$$\text{Orthogonal Matrix} \times \text{Diagonal Matrix} \times \text{Orthogonal Matrix}$$

There are many ways to discover the SVD. We’ll follow the most geometric one: the search for maximum stretch. Along the way, you’ll see how the other perspectives—like matching bases and analyzing Gram matrices—naturally fall out.

The Philosophy: Why Eigenvectors Fail Us

For symmetric square matrices, eigenvectors work beautifully because applying the matrix keeps the vector in the same space—the direction is preserved up to a scale ($Ax = \lambda x$). But for a general rectangular $m \times n$ matrix $A$, this idea breaks down. $A$ takes an input vector $v$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and outputs a vector $Av$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$. They don't live in the same dimension, so asking if $Av$ points in the "same direction" as $v$ is geometrically meaningless.

This is exactly why eigen-decomposition fails in general—but the SVD succeeds. Instead of insisting on one basis that works before and after, the SVD allows two different bases, one for each space.

The Perfect Coordinates
$$A v_i = \sigma_i\, u_i$$

The SVD is not just a decomposition. It is the discovery of the perfect input coordinates (the $v_i$) and the perfect output coordinates (the $u_i$) in which the matrix $A$ becomes as simple as possible—purely diagonal scaling.

The Search for Maximum Stretch

We are looking for the directions that get stretched the most by $A$. So, which unit vector $v$ makes the length of the output, $\|Av\|$, as large as possible? Let's look at the squared length:

$$\|A v\|_2^2 = (A v)^T (A v) = v^T A^T A v$$

Look at what just appeared: $A^T A$, the row Gram matrix. Even if $A$ is rectangular and messy, $A^T A$ is guaranteed to be a square, symmetric, positive semi-definite matrix. In linear algebra, a quadratic form like $v^T (A^T A) v$ is maximized exactly when $v$ is chosen to be the eigenvector of $A^T A$ corresponding to its largest eigenvalue:

$$A^T A v = \lambda_{\max} v$$

These vectors $v$ are not the eigenvectors of $A$ (which don't exist for rectangular matrices), but the eigenvectors of $A^T A$. The maximum stretch we achieve is the square root of that eigenvalue. We call this stretch the singular value, $\sigma$:

$$\sigma = \sqrt{\lambda_{\max}}, \qquad A^T A v = \sigma^2 v \quad \text{(Right singular vectors } v\text{)}$$

This vector $v$ is our first Right Singular Vector. It is called "right" because it will sit on the right side of the SVD factorization. We continue to the next-largest eigenvalue, finding the direction of second-greatest stretch, and so on through all $r$ non-zero eigenvalues.

What Happens After the Stretch?

We have found our input directions $v$, and we know $A^T A v = \sigma^2 v$. But what happens to these vectors when they land in the column space? We defined our target relationship as $Av = \sigma u$.

If $v$ is a direction of pure stretching, then its image $Av$ should also behave like a "pure direction" in the output space. That suggests $Av$ itself should be an eigenvector of something natural in the output space—namely the column Gram matrix, $AA^T$.

Let's trace the math to verify this. Take our equation for $v$ and multiply both sides by $A$:

$$A(A^T A v) = A(\sigma^2 v)$$

Regroup the parentheses on the left side:

$$(A A^T)(A v) = \sigma^2 (A v)$$

Now, substitute our core definition $Av = \sigma u$ into both sides:

$$(A A^T)(\sigma u) = \sigma^2 (\sigma u)$$

Cancel out a $\sigma$ from both sides:

$$A A^T u = \sigma^2 u \quad \text{(Left singular vectors } u\text{)}$$

The output vector $u$ is exactly the eigenvector of $AA^T$ with the eigenvalue $\sigma^2$. We call $u$ the Left Singular Vector.

The Grand Synthesis: $A = U \Sigma V^T$

The eigenbasis $V$ (from $A^T A$) gets mapped by $A$ into the eigenbasis $U$ (from $A A^T$), scaled by the singular values. In matrix form, this is $A V = U \Sigma$. Rearranging by multiplying by $V^T$ gives us the full singular value decomposition:

The Singular Value Decomposition
$$A = U \Sigma V^T$$

Geometrically, every matrix transformation can be understood in three clean steps:

  1. $V^T$: rotate the input space so we are looking along the special directions $v_i$. (Mathematically, multiplying an input vector by $V^T$ takes the dot product with every basis vector $v_i$, projecting it into the $V$ coordinate system).
  2. $\Sigma$: stretch or compress along perpendicular axes. (No mixing at all).
  3. $U$: rotate the result into its final orientation.

The miracle of the SVD is that all the complexity of the matrix is captured by rotations plus independent scalings—no shearing, no entanglement.

Why $A^T A$ and $A A^T$ Were Inevitable

If we want to verify how this connects back to our symmetric Gram matrices, we can just plug the SVD into $A^T A$ and $A A^T$:

$$A^T A = (U\Sigma V^T)^T(U\Sigma V^T) = V \Sigma^T U^T U \Sigma V^T = V \Sigma^T \Sigma V^T$$
$$A A^T = (U\Sigma V^T)(U\Sigma V^T)^T = U \Sigma V^T V \Sigma^T U^T = U \Sigma \Sigma^T U^T$$

Because $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal ($U^T U = I$ and $V^T V = I$), the equations collapse exactly to the standard symmetric diagonalizations — $V$ diagonalizes $A^T A$ and $U$ diagonalizes $A A^T$, with eigenvalues $\sigma_i^2$ in both cases.

The Ultimate Map of the Four Fundamental Subspaces

The SVD doesn’t just tell us what these subspaces are — it gives the best possible orthonormal basis for each one, aligned exactly with how the matrix actually acts. Suppose $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix of rank $r$.

  • The Row Space $C(A^T)$: The first $r$ columns of $V$ ($v_1, \dots, v_r$) form an orthonormal basis for the row space.
  • The Null Space $N(A)$: The remaining $n - r$ columns of $V$ ($v_{r+1}, \dots, v_n$) form an orthonormal basis for the null space. These are the vectors that $A$ crushes to zero — their singular values are $0$.
  • The Column Space $C(A)$: The first $r$ columns of $U$ ($u_1, \dots, u_r$) form an orthonormal basis for the column space. These are precisely the directions that survive under the transformation.
  • The Left Null Space $N(A^T)$: The remaining $m - r$ columns of $U$ ($u_{r+1}, \dots, u_m$) form an orthonormal basis for the left null space.
Worked Example 1: A Full-Rank 2×2 Matrix

Let us take $A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$. This matrix is invertible with rank 2, so we expect two non-zero singular values.

Step 1: Compute $A^T A$ and find the right singular vectors $V$.

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix}4 & -3 \\ 4 & 3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}32 & 0 \\ 0 & 18\end{bmatrix}$$

$A^T A$ comes out diagonal. The eigenvalues are $\sigma_1^2 = 32$ and $\sigma_2^2 = 18$, read directly off the diagonal.

The eigenvectors (columns of $V$) are found from the null spaces of $(A^TA - \sigma_i^2 I)$. Here, the eigenvectors are along the directions $(1,1)$ and $(1,-1)$, which we normalize to unit vectors by dividing by $\sqrt{2}$:

$$v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix}, \qquad V = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}$$

Step 2: Compute $A A^T$ and find the left singular vectors $U$.

$$A A^T = \begin{bmatrix}4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}4 & -3 \\ 4 & 3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}32 & 0 \\ 0 & 18\end{bmatrix}$$

Notice that $AA^T$ also comes out diagonal with eigenvalues $32$ and $18$. This is no accident: $A^T A$ and $AA^T$ always share the same non-zero eigenvalues. The eigenvectors of $AA^T$ give the columns of $U$: $u_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}$ and $u_2 = \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}$.

Step 3: Determine the correct signs using $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$.

The sign of each $u_i$ must be consistent with $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$. The eigenvectors of $AA^T$ only give us the direction — we must verify the sign against the actual transformation. Let's check $v_2$:

$$A v_2 = \begin{bmatrix}4 & 4 \\ -3 & 3\end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}0\\-6\end{bmatrix} = \sqrt{18} \begin{bmatrix}0\\-1\end{bmatrix}$$

So $u_2 = \begin{bmatrix}0\\-1\end{bmatrix}$, not $\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}$. The sign flipped. Always determine $u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i}Av_i$ to get the correct sign, rather than reading it directly from the eigenvectors of $AA^T$.

The complete SVD:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\sqrt{32} & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{18}\end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{bmatrix}$$
Worked Example 2: A Rank-1 Matrix

Let's solidify this with a singular $2 \times 2$ matrix with rank 1:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 \\ 8 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

Because it's rank 1, the row space is spanned by the rows $[4, 3]$ and $[8, 6]$. Since $[8, 6] = 2[4, 3]$, they span the same 1D line — all multiples of $(4, 3)$. To find our first input vector $v_1$, we normalize it to a unit vector: $\|(4,3)\| = 5$, so $v_1 = (0.8, 0.6)$. The null space is the perpendicular line, giving us $v_2 = (0.6, -0.8)$. These form our matrix $V$.

The column space is also a 1D line — multiples of $(4, 8)$, or simplified, $(1, 2)$. Normalizing gives $u_1 = \bigl(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{5}}, \tfrac{2}{\sqrt{5}}\bigr)$. The left null space is the perpendicular vector $u_2 = \bigl(\tfrac{2}{\sqrt{5}}, \tfrac{-1}{\sqrt{5}}\bigr)$. These form our matrix $U$, which we can write as $\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 2 & -1\end{bmatrix}$.

For the singular value, we compute $A^T A$:

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix}4 & 8 \\ 3 & 6\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}4 & 3 \\ 8 & 6\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}80 & 60 \\ 60 & 45\end{bmatrix}$$

This rank-1 matrix has trace $= 80 + 45 = 125$. Since the rank is 1, one eigenvalue is $0$, so the non-zero eigenvalue must be the entire trace: $\lambda = 125$. The singular value is $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{125} = 5\sqrt{5}$, and $\sigma_2 = 0$. The zeros slot perfectly into $\Sigma$ for the null space dimensions:

$$A = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 2 & -1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}5\sqrt{5} & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}0.8 & 0.6 \\ 0.6 & -0.8\end{bmatrix}$$

This means the entire matrix can be written as a single outer product: $A = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T$. A rank-1 matrix is nothing more than one direction in input space mapped to one direction in output space.

The Optimal Low-Rank Approximation

Because the SVD separates the geometry (the orthogonal matrices $U$ and $V$) from the magnitude (the singular values $\sigma$), it gives us a profound way to compress information. If we carry out the matrix multiplication $A = U \Sigma V^T$ column by column, it expands into a sum of rank-one matrices:

$$A = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T + \sigma_2 u_2 v_2^T + \dots + \sigma_r u_r v_r^T$$
$$A = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

By convention, singular values are always sorted in descending order ($\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \dots$). The first term $\sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T$ contains the single most important piece of information in the matrix. The second term is the second most important.

If we want to compress a massive dataset, we simply stop the sum early. Truncating the small $\sigma_i$ terms throws away the mathematical "noise" and leaves us with the absolute best low-rank approximation of $A$ geometrically possible. Keeping only the first $k$ terms gives the closest rank-$k$ matrix to $A$ — not just intuitively, but provably optimal among all possible rank-$k$ matrices. This profound result is known as the Eckart–Young–Mirsky theorem, and it is the foundational algorithm powering everything from modern image compression to digital recommendation systems.

In summary:
Eigen-decomposition is "change basis → scale → return to the same basis."
The SVD is "change basis → scale → change to a different basis."
