The "Cold Start" Problem and the Compass Analogy

One way to understand the limitation of a purely autoregressive view is to think about drawing a circle. Suppose you first decide the center of the circle and its radius. Conceptually, this high-level decision happens before any individual pixel is drawn. Once these global factors are fixed, you place a compass at the center, set the radius, and begin tracing the circumference. The very first visible pixel may appear as $x_1$ in an autoregressive sequence, but its placement is not truly "random"—it is already entirely determined by the prior, hidden choice of center and radius.

This reveals a critical distinction: the most meaningful semantic information does not reside in the first visible pixel itself, even if subsequent pixels allow us to reverse-engineer it. The global structure exists prior to the pixel-level generation process.

The Bayesian Shift: This is precisely the perspective of a latent variable model. A hidden variable, $Z$, captures abstract factors such as shape, position, and scale. The image is then generated from this blueprint through a directed Bayesian process:
$$Z \rightarrow X$$

In this view, even the first pixel $x_1$ is better understood as being causally driven by $Z$, rather than emerging independently from the autoregressive chain.

Watch this distinction in action. First, observe the autoregressive approach. Notice how the shape is distributed across individual boundary pixels, and the model must reconstruct the circle by sequentially activating one local piece after another, starting from an unanchored first pixel.

Now compare this with the latent variable model. This represents the compass analogy perfectly. The entire shape is explained using a compact hidden code $Z$. Because the model possesses this semantic intent first, the latent variables instantaneously dictate the global structure of $X$.
