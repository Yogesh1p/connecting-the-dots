Diagonalisation

When a matrix has enough independent eigenvectors, the linear transformation can be expressed in its natural coordinate system: the basis formed by its eigenvectors.

Key idea :

In the eigenvector basis, the matrix no longer mixes coordinates. Each eigendirection scales independently by its own eigenvalue.

\[ AX = X\Lambda \]
\[ \Lambda= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2 & \cdots & 0\\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix} \]

For a \(2\times2\) matrix, this columnwise scaling becomes:

\[ A \begin{bmatrix} \vec{x}_1 & \vec{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1\vec{x}_1 & \lambda_2\vec{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \vec{x}_1 & \vec{x}_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0\\ 0 & \lambda_2 \end{bmatrix} \]

If the eigenvectors are linearly independent, \(X\) is invertible. Multiplying by \(X^{-1}\) changes into the eigenvector basis:

Diagonalisation
\[ A=X\Lambda X^{-1} \]

In this basis, the matrix acts independently along each eigendirection.

When is this possible?

A matrix is diagonalizable exactly when it has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors.

\[ \sum_i \mathrm{GM}(\lambda_i)=n \]

Equivalently, for every eigenvalue, \(\mathrm{GM}(\lambda)=\mathrm{AM}(\lambda)\). Distinct eigenvalues always guarantee this.

When is this not possible?

Consider

\[ A= \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \]

Its characteristic polynomial is \((2-\lambda)^2\), so the eigenvalue \(\lambda=2\) has \(\mathrm{AM}(2)=2\).

But solving \((A-2I)x=0\) gives only one independent eigenvector direction:

\[ \mathrm{GM}(2)=1 \]

Since \(\mathrm{GM}(2)=1<2=\mathrm{AM}(2)\), the matrix does not have enough eigenvectors, so it is not diagonalizable.

The real power of diagonalisation appears when taking powers:

\[ A^k=X\Lambda^kX^{-1} \]

So instead of multiplying the full matrix repeatedly, we only raise each eigenvalue on the diagonal:

\[ \Lambda^k= \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1^k,\dots,\lambda_n^k) \]

Rank tells you how many dimensions survive under \(A\), not which special directions survive unchanged.

  • If \(\operatorname{rank}(A)=r\), then nullity is \(n-r\), giving \(n-r\) eigenvectors with \(\lambda=0\)
  • Rank does not determine diagonalisability

Symmetric Matrices and the Spectral Theorem

The first remarkable fact about a real symmetric matrix \(S=S^T\) is that its eigenvalues can never be complex. Symmetry forces every eigenvalue to be real.

Real eigenvalues

If \(S=S^T\) and \(Sx=\lambda x\), then \(\lambda\in\mathbb{R}\).

Proof

Start from

\[ Sx=\lambda x \]

Since \(S\) is real,

\[ \overline{S}=S \]

taking conjugates gives

\[ S\bar{x}=\bar{\lambda}\bar{x} \]

Multiply on the left by \(\bar{x}^T\):

\[ \bar{x}^TSx=\lambda\bar{x}^Tx \]

By symmetry, the same left-hand side also equals

\[ \bar{x}^TSx=\bar{\lambda}\bar{x}^Tx \]

Since \(\bar{x}^Tx>0\),

\[ \lambda=\bar{\lambda} \]

Hence \(\lambda\) is real.

Orthogonal eigenvectors — only for distinct eigenvalues

For a real symmetric matrix, eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are always orthogonal.

Important: This orthogonality guarantee applies only when the two eigenvectors belong to different eigenvalues (\(\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2\)). When an eigenvalue is repeated, its eigenspace may have dimension greater than one, and eigenvectors within that eigenspace are not automatically orthogonal to each other — you must apply Gram–Schmidt to orthogonalise them. The Spectral Theorem guarantees you can always choose an orthonormal basis for each eigenspace, but the raw eigenvectors you first solve for may not be.

Proof

Suppose

\[ Sx=\lambda_1x,\qquad Sy=\lambda_2y \]

where \(\lambda_1\neq\lambda_2\). Take the dot product of the first equation with \(y\):

\[ y^TSx=\lambda_1 y^Tx \]

Using symmetry \(S^T=S\), we may shift \(S\) to the other side:

\[ y^TSx=x^TS^Ty=x^TSy=\lambda_2 x^Ty \]

Therefore

\[ \lambda_1 x^Ty=\lambda_2 x^Ty \]

Since \(\lambda_1\neq\lambda_2\), this forces

\[ x^Ty=0 \]

So the eigenvectors are perpendicular.

Worked example: repeated eigenvalue where raw eigenvectors are not orthogonal

Take the \(3\times3\) matrix of all ones with zeros on the diagonal:

\[ S = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

This matrix is symmetric (\(S = S^T\)), so the Spectral Theorem applies. Its characteristic polynomial gives eigenvalues

\[ \lambda = 2 \quad (\text{simple}), \qquad \lambda = -1 \quad (\text{repeated, multiplicity } 2) \]

Step 1 — Eigenvector for \(\lambda = 2\).

Solve \((S - 2I)x = 0\). The eigenvector is

\[ v_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 2 — Eigenspace for \(\lambda = -1\).

Solve \((S + I)x = 0\). Adding \(I\) to \(S\) gives a matrix where every row is \([1,1,1]\), so the only constraint is \(x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 0\). This is a two-dimensional eigenspace (two free variables). Two natural basis vectors are:

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Both satisfy \(Sv_1 = -v_1\) and \(Sv_2 = -v_2\), so both are valid eigenvectors for \(\lambda = -1\).

Step 3 — Check: are \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) orthogonal to each other?

\[ v_1 \cdot v_2 = (1)(1) + (-1)(0) + (0)(-1) = 1 \neq 0 \]

They are not orthogonal. This is the key point: both live in the same eigenspace (\(\lambda = -1\)), so the theorem gives no automatic orthogonality guarantee between them.

Step 4 — Verify cross-eigenspace orthogonality is automatic.

Now check \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) against \(v_3\) (which belongs to the different eigenvalue \(\lambda = 2\)):

\[ v_1 \cdot v_3 = 1 + (-1) + 0 = 0 \checkmark \qquad v_2 \cdot v_3 = 1 + 0 + (-1) = 0 \checkmark \]

As the theorem guarantees, any eigenvector of \(\lambda = -1\) is automatically orthogonal to the eigenvector of \(\lambda = 2\), with no extra work needed.

Step 5 — Gram–Schmidt inside the repeated eigenspace.

To get an orthogonal pair within the \(\lambda = -1\) eigenspace, apply Gram–Schmidt. Keep \(v_1\) as is, then subtract its projection from \(v_2\):

\[ u_1 = v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \]
\[ u_2 = v_2 - \frac{v_2 \cdot u_1}{\|u_1\|^2}\, u_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{2} \\ \tfrac{1}{2} \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Now check:

\[ u_1 \cdot u_2 = \tfrac{1}{2} + \bigl(-\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr) + 0 = 0 \checkmark \]

The three vectors \(u_1,\, u_2,\, v_3\) now form an orthogonal eigenbasis for \(S\), confirming the Spectral Theorem — but the orthogonality within the repeated eigenspace required explicit Gram–Schmidt, not the theorem itself.

\[ \underbrace{u_1, u_2}_{\lambda=-1,\ \text{orthogonalised by Gram–Schmidt}} \qquad \underbrace{v_3}_{\lambda=2,\ \text{automatically orthogonal to both}} \]
Symmetric matrices are always diagonalizable

Repeated eigenvalues may destroy diagonalisation for general matrices, but this never happens for symmetric matrices.

Proof idea 1: perturbation intuition

Slightly perturb the matrix by adding a tiny diagonal term

\[ S+\operatorname{diag}(c,2c,\dots,nc) \]

For very small \(c\), the repeated eigenvalues split apart. The perturbed symmetric matrix now has distinct eigenvalues, hence a full orthonormal eigenbasis.

As \(c\to 0\), these orthonormal eigenvectors converge to an orthonormal basis for the original matrix.

This gives the right intuition, though by itself it is not a fully rigorous proof.

Proof 2: Schur factorization (rigorous)

Every square matrix admits the Schur factorization

\[ A=QTQ^T \]

where \(Q\) is orthogonal and \(T\) is upper triangular. Applying this to a symmetric matrix gives

\[ S=QTQ^T \]

Taking transposes and using \(S=S^T\):

\[ T=Q^TSQ,\qquad T^T=Q^TS^TQ=T \]

So \(T\) is both upper triangular and symmetric, which forces it to be diagonal:

\[ T=\Lambda \]

Hence

\[ S=Q\Lambda Q^T \]

Therefore symmetric matrices always have a full orthonormal eigenbasis.

Proof 3: one eigenvector at a time

Consider the quadratic form

\[ f(x)=x^TSx \]

over all unit vectors \(\|x\|=1\). Since the unit sphere is compact, \(f(x)\) attains a maximum at some unit vector \(q_1\).

To maximize \(x^TSx\) subject to \(x^Tx=1\), use a Lagrange multiplier:

\[ \nabla(x^TSx)=\lambda \nabla(x^Tx) \]

Because \(S\) is symmetric,

\[ 2Sx=2\lambda x \]

so

\[ Sq_1=\lambda_1 q_1 \]

Thus the maximizing direction is an eigenvector.

Now search for the next maximum among unit vectors satisfying

\[ x^Tq_1=0 \]

This constrained set is again compact, so the maximum exists at a vector \(q_2\), which by the same Lagrange multiplier argument satisfies

\[ Sq_2=\lambda_2 q_2 \]

Continue inductively: after finding \(q_1,\dots,q_k\), maximize over unit vectors satisfying

\[ x^Tq_1=\cdots=x^Tq_k=0 \]

Each step produces a new eigenvector orthogonal to all previous ones.

After \(n\) steps, we obtain an orthonormal basis

\[ q_1,\dots,q_n \]

consisting entirely of eigenvectors. Therefore

\[ S=Q\Lambda Q^T \]

which proves the spectral theorem.

Spectral Theorem

Every real symmetric matrix \(S=S^T\) admits an orthogonal diagonalization. There exists an orthogonal matrix \(Q\) whose columns are orthonormal eigenvectors of \(S\), such that

\[ S = Q\Lambda Q^T \]

where \(\Lambda\) is the diagonal matrix of real eigenvalues.

This is the symmetric version of diagonalisation. The eigenvector matrix becomes orthogonal:

\[ Q^TQ=I,\qquad Q^{-1}=Q^T \]

So the matrix action becomes: rotate, stretch, rotate back.

Geometric interpretation
\[ S = Q\Lambda Q^T \]

First rotate into the eigenvector coordinates, then stretch each orthogonal axis by its eigenvalue, then rotate back.

Expanding the matrix product column-by-row gives the spectral expansion:

\[ S=\lambda_1 q_1 q_1^T+\cdots+\lambda_n q_n q_n^T \]
Key consequences
  • All eigenvalues of \(S\) are real
  • All symmetric matrices are diagonalizable
  • Repeated eigenvalues still give enough eigenvectors (the eigenspace always has the right dimension, and an orthonormal basis can always be chosen within it using Gram–Schmidt)
  • Eigenvectors belonging to distinct eigenvalues are automatically orthogonal; eigenvectors within the same repeated eigenspace are not automatically orthogonal and must be orthogonalised separately
  • The number of positive eigenvalues equals the number of positive pivots
Antisymmetric contrast

If \(A=-A^T\), the situation changes completely: eigenvalues become purely imaginary and eigenvectors are orthonormal only over the complex field.
