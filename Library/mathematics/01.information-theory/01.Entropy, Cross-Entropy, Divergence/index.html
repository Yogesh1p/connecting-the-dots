Surprise: The Foundation of Information

Every concept in this article builds on a single, concrete intuition: how surprised should you be when an event occurs?

If you flip a fair coin and it lands heads, that is unremarkable — you expected roughly even odds. But if a weather forecast confidently predicts sunshine and it snows, that is genuinely surprising. We want a function that captures this feeling precisely, in a way that is mathematically useful. We define:

$$\text{Surprise}(x) = -\log P(x)$$

The two boundary cases confirm the intuition:

  • $P(x) \to 1$ (a near-certain event): $-\log P(x) \to 0$. No surprise.
  • $P(x) \to 0$ (a near-impossible event): $-\log P(x) \to \infty$. Extreme surprise.

Why a Logarithm?

The choice of logarithm is not cosmetic — it enforces a critical structural property. Consider two independent events $A$ and $B$. Their probabilities multiply:

$$P(A, B) = P(A)\,P(B)$$

Intuitively, the information content of two independent events should add — learning that it rained in London and that a coin landed heads gives you the sum of both pieces of information, not their product. The logarithm is the unique function that converts multiplication into addition:

$$-\log P(A, B) = -\log P(A) - \log P(B)$$

This additive structure is foundational to information theory, compression, and machine learning optimization alike. (For instance, $\frac{1}{P(x)}$ would also grow as probability shrinks, but it multiplies for independent events rather than adding — making it mathematically intractable in downstream formulae.)

The Link to Compression

There is a concrete, operational meaning to surprise: it is the optimal code length. If you are designing a binary code to transmit outcomes, the most efficient scheme assigns shorter codes to likely events and longer codes to rare ones. The Shannon source coding theorem proves that the minimum average code length for an event with probability $P(x)$ is exactly $-\log_2 P(x)$ bits. Surprise, measured in bits, is literally the minimum description length of an event.

Entropy: Expected Surprise Over a Distribution

A single event's surprise tells you about one outcome. But to characterise the overall unpredictability of a random variable, you take the expectation of surprise across all possible outcomes under the true distribution $P$:

Entropy
$$H(P) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P}[-\log P(x)] = -\sum_x P(x) \log P(x)$$

Entropy is the average number of bits needed to describe outcomes drawn from $P$. It measures irreducible uncertainty — the minimum compression achievable when you know the true distribution.

Two extremes are instructive. A fair coin ($p = 0.5$) has maximum binary entropy: $H = 1$ bit — you cannot compress the outcome at all. A loaded coin that always lands heads ($p = 1$) has $H = 0$ bits: every outcome is known in advance, so no information is transmitted.

Cross-Entropy: The Cost of a Wrong Model

Entropy assumes you encode events using their true probabilities. But in machine learning, you never have the true distribution — you only have a model's approximation $Q$. Cross-entropy measures the average surprise when reality operates under $P$ but your encoding scheme is designed for $Q$:

$$H(P, Q) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P}[-\log Q(x)] = -\sum_x P(x) \log Q(x)$$

The separation of duties here is deliberate and important: $P$ governs how often events occur (so it weights the expectation), while $Q$ governs how surprised you are when each event occurs (so it appears inside the log). Cross-entropy therefore combines two factors: the world's inherent randomness, and the extra inefficiency introduced by using the wrong model.

Crucially, $H(P, Q) \geq H(P)$ always. You can never do better than the true distribution's entropy; mismatched assumptions only add cost.

KL Divergence: Isolating the Model's Error

Since $H(P)$ is fixed by reality and beyond any model's control, the quantity that actually matters for optimisation is the excess — how much extra surprise does the wrong model introduce? This is the Kullback-Leibler divergence:

KL Divergence
$$D_{KL}(P \parallel Q) = H(P, Q) - H(P) = \sum_x P(x) \log \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$$

$D_{KL}(P \parallel Q) \geq 0$ always, with equality if and only if $P = Q$ everywhere. It measures the avoidable surprise — the portion of total surprise that comes solely from the model's incorrect assumptions, not from the world's inherent randomness.

Minimising cross-entropy and minimising KL divergence are equivalent optimisation problems (since $H(P)$ is constant with respect to model parameters). In practice, cross-entropy is easier to compute and is used as the training loss directly.

Asymmetry and Its Consequences

KL divergence has a property that is easy to overlook but has major practical implications:

$$D_{KL}(P \parallel Q) \neq D_{KL}(Q \parallel P)$$

The direction matters because it changes which errors are penalised heavily. In forward KL ($D_{KL}(P \parallel Q)$), the expectation is taken under $P$. Wherever $P(x) > 0$ but $Q(x) \approx 0$, the term $P(x)\log\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ becomes enormous. The model is severely punished for any region of the true distribution it fails to cover. This forces $Q$ to spread mass broadly, producing mode-covering behaviour — the model prefers blurry, averaged outputs over omitting true modes.

In reverse KL ($D_{KL}(Q \parallel P)$), the expectation is under $Q$. Wherever $P(x) \approx 0$ but $Q(x) > 0$, the term $Q(x)\log\frac{Q(x)}{P(x)}$ blows up. The model is punished for placing mass outside the true distribution, so it retreats to safe, high-density regions — producing mode-seeking behaviour and, in practice, mode collapse in generative models.

Standard supervised learning uses forward KL (or equivalently, cross-entropy). Variational inference often minimises reverse KL. Understanding which direction you are optimising is essential when interpreting a model's failure modes.

KL divergence is also not a true metric: it is asymmetric, does not satisfy the triangle inequality, and diverges to infinity when $Q$ assigns zero probability to a region where $P$ is positive. These limitations motivated alternatives such as the Jensen-Shannon divergence (which symmetrises KL) and Wasserstein distance.

Wasserstein Distance: Geometry Over Point-Wise Comparison

Both entropy and KL divergence compare probability values locally — at each point $x$ in isolation. They have no notion of the underlying space's geometry. Consider two distributions, each a spike, separated in space: KL divergence between them is infinite the moment the supports do not overlap, even if the spikes are only a tiny distance apart.

Wasserstein distance (the Earth Mover Distance) instead asks: what is the minimum total work required to physically transport probability mass from one distribution to the other? Work is measured as mass times distance, so nearby distributions are always close, regardless of support overlap.

This geometric property provides meaningful, non-degenerate gradients even when $P$ and $Q$ barely overlap — a regime where KL divergence produces zero or infinite gradients and stalls training. Wasserstein distance was central to the stability improvements of Wasserstein GANs and continues to inform modern generative modelling.

Grounding It in Machine Learning: Binary Cross-Entropy

The concepts above are not abstract — they converge directly on one of the most common loss functions in deep learning. In binary classification, the model outputs a single probability $\hat{p} = P(y=1 \mid x; \theta)$, and the true label $y \in \{0, 1\}$ defines a deterministic distribution over two outcomes. The true distribution $P$ and predicted distribution $Q$ are:

  • $P = (y,\; 1-y)$, which is $(1, 0)$ when $y=1$ and $(0, 1)$ when $y=0$.
  • $Q = (\hat{p},\; 1-\hat{p})$ always.

Substituting into $H(P, Q) = -\sum_x P(x)\log Q(x)$ and noting that one term is always zero (since $y$ is 0 or 1):

Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) Loss
$$L_i = -\bigl[y_i \log \hat{p}_i + (1 - y_i)\log (1 - \hat{p}_i)\bigr]$$

This formula has an exact probabilistic interpretation. The true label $y_i$ follows a Bernoulli distribution. The probability that the model assigns to the actual observed label is $\hat{p}_i^{\,y_i}(1-\hat{p}_i)^{1-y_i}$. Over a dataset of $N$ independent examples, the joint likelihood is:

$$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \hat{p}_i^{\,y_i}(1-\hat{p}_i)^{1-y_i}$$

Taking the logarithm (to convert the product to a sum, and to avoid numerical underflow) and negating (to turn maximisation into minimisation) recovers exactly the BCE loss. BCE is not an ad hoc engineering choice — it is the negative log likelihood of a Bernoulli model. Minimising BCE is precisely maximum likelihood estimation.

The Monte Carlo Connection

There is one subtle point worth making explicit. The theoretical cross-entropy is:

$$H(P, Q) = -\sum_x P(x)\log Q(x)$$

But the training loss averages uniformly over samples: $\frac{1}{N}\sum_i L_i$. Where did the $P(x)$ weighting go? It is implicitly present. Because each training example $(x_i, y_i)$ is drawn from the true data distribution, common events appear more frequently in the dataset. The empirical frequency of each example is the Monte Carlo estimate of $P(x)$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim P}[-\log Q(y|x)] \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_i L_i$$

This is a direct consequence of the law of large numbers. Averaging individual losses over a dataset exactly recovers the theoretical cross-entropy in the limit of large $N$.

Why Cross-Entropy Produces Effective Gradients

A loss function is only as good as the gradients it produces. Two properties make BCE particularly effective for training neural networks.

Confident Mistakes Are Harshly Penalised

If the true label is $y = 1$ but the model predicts $\hat{p} \to 0$, the loss $-\log(\hat{p}) \to \infty$. This creates extreme pressure against confident errors, which is exactly the right inductive bias for a probabilistic classifier.

Gradients Through Softmax Are Algebraically Clean

Neural networks output logits $z_i$, converted to probabilities by softmax: $\hat{y}_i = e^{z_i}/\sum_j e^{z_j}$. Computing $\partial L / \partial z_i$ through the chain rule naively looks messy — the gradient of cross-entropy with respect to $\hat{y}$ is $-1/\hat{y}$, which explodes near zero. However, the sigmoid or softmax derivative contributes a compensating $\hat{y}(1-\hat{y})$ term. These factors cancel exactly, leaving:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_i} = \hat{y}_i - y_i$$

The gradient is simply prediction minus truth. The network directly corrects its probability toward the true distribution, with no vanishing or exploding terms. This algebraic cancellation is one of the deepest reasons softmax and cross-entropy are paired so consistently in deep learning.

Why Not Mean Squared Error?

MSE loss $(\hat{y} - y)^2$ assumes a Gaussian noise model and is appropriate for regression. Applied to classification through a sigmoid, its gradient picks up an additional $\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$ factor. Near probability extremes (where the sigmoid saturates), this derivative is nearly zero, causing gradients to vanish and learning to stall. BCE avoids this saturation because the problematic sigmoid terms cancel in the chain rule. Additionally, MSE penalises confident mistakes only quadratically, while BCE penalises them logarithmically — much more aggressively — producing better-calibrated probability outputs.

The Frequency Bias of Cross-Entropy

↗ The Semantic Gap

The Monte Carlo interpretation carries an important limitation. Because the dataset's empirical frequencies act as $P(x)$, frequent samples dominate the loss. A rare but semantically rich example — the kind that might encode an unusual reasoning pattern or a long-tail phenomenon — contributes almost nothing to the aggregate gradient signal.

The model is strictly optimising counting statistics, not the underlying structure of the world. This is why raw cross-entropy alone is often insufficient for deep generalisation or AGI-level reasoning: it is well-calibrated for common cases but structurally blind to rare, high-information events. Addressing this requires intentional reweighting, curriculum learning, or alternative objectives — not simply more data.

Beyond KL: Modern Generative Objectives

KL divergence — and by extension cross-entropy — underpins most discriminative learning. But in generative modelling, its limitations become acute. Forward KL encourages blurry, mode-covering outputs. Reverse KL encourages mode collapse. And KL divergence is undefined when distributions have non-overlapping support, which is typical in high dimensions.

Wasserstein distance addresses the support problem by incorporating geometry. Modern diffusion models and score-based generative models sidestep explicit divergence minimisation altogether, instead learning denoising objectives or the gradient of the log data density (the score function). These methods achieve more stable training and higher sample quality precisely because they operate on the geometry and dynamics of probability distributions, rather than comparing probability values pointwise.

The arc from surprise, through entropy and KL divergence, to score-based modelling reflects a broader maturation in how the field thinks about probability: from matching numbers, to understanding the shape of the space those numbers live in.
