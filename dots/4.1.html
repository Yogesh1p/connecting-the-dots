Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero: $v \cdot w = v^T w = 0$. Geometrically, this forms a right angle, meaning the Pythagorean theorem applies. For a right triangle with vector sides $v$ and $w$, the hypotenuse is $v + w$.

Orthogonal Vectors

Two vectors $v$ and $w$ are orthogonal if and only if:

$$ \|v\|^2 + \|w\|^2 = \|v + w\|^2 $$

Expanding the right side gives $(v + w)^T(v + w) = v^Tv + v^Tw + w^Tv + w^Tw$. This reduces to the left side precisely when the cross terms vanish: $v^Tw = w^Tv = 0$.

When analyzing $Ax = b$, the column space and the null space immediately prove useful. However, introducing $A^T$ uncovers two more fundamental subspaces. Together, these four fundamental subspaces reveal the true geometric reality of matrix multiplication. The key insight is that these spaces fit together at $90^\circ$ angles.

1. Definition of Orthogonal Subspaces

It is not just individual vectors that can be orthogonal; entire subspaces can be orthogonal to one another.

Orthogonal Subspaces

Two subspaces $V$ and $W$ of a vector space are orthogonal if every vector $v$ in $V$ is perpendicular to every vector $w$ in $W$:

$$ v^T w = 0 \quad \text{for all } v \in V \text{ and all } w \in W. $$

Example 1 (Orthogonal): Consider the floor of a room (extended to infinity) as a subspace $V$, and the vertical line where two walls meet as a subspace $W$. Every vector pointing straight up the wall is perpendicular to every vector lying flat on the floor. These subspaces are orthogonal.

Example 2 (Not Orthogonal): Two adjacent walls in a room look perpendicular, but as subspaces, they are not orthogonal. The meeting line belongs to both walls, and a non-zero vector on that line cannot be perpendicular to itself. In fact, if a vector is in two orthogonal subspaces, it must be the zero vector because $v^Tv = 0$.

2. The First Pair: Null Space and Row Space

Zero is the only point where the null space meets the row space. More importantly, they meet exactly at $90^\circ$. This key fact comes directly from the definition of the null space: $Ax = 0$.

Orthogonality in $\mathbb{R}^n$

Every vector $x$ in the null space is perpendicular to every row of $A$, because $Ax = 0$.
The null space $N(A)$ and the row space $C(A^T)$ are orthogonal subspaces in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

To see why $x$ is perpendicular to the rows, look at how matrix multiplication executes $Ax = 0$. Each row multiplies $x$:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} \text{row } 1 \\ \vdots \\ \text{row } m \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

The first equation states that row 1 is perpendicular to $x$. The last equation states that row $m$ is perpendicular to $x$. Because $x$ has a zero dot product with every individual row, it is also perpendicular to every linear combination of those rows. Therefore, the entire row space is orthogonal to the null space.

3. The Second Pair: Left Null Space and Column Space

By applying the exact same logic to the transpose of the matrix, $A^T$, we find the relationship for the remaining two fundamental subspaces.

Orthogonality in $\mathbb{R}^m$

Every vector $y$ in the null space of $A^T$ is perpendicular to every column of $A$.
The left null space $N(A^T)$ and the column space $C(A)$ are orthogonal subspaces in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

Looking at $A^Ty = 0$, each column of $A$ (which acts as a row in $A^T$) multiplies $y$ to yield zero. Consequently, any vector $y$ in the left null space is perpendicular to every combination of the columns.

4. Orthogonal Complements

The fundamental subspaces are more than just orthogonal; their dimensions perfectly complete the whole space. Two lines could be perpendicular in $\mathbb{R}^3$, but their dimensions ($1 + 1 = 2$) don't fill the 3D space. For an $m \times n$ matrix $A$, the correct dimensions must add up to $n$ (for the input space) and $m$ (for the output space).

Orthogonal Complement

The orthogonal complement of a subspace $V$ contains every vector that is perpendicular to $V$. This orthogonal subspace is denoted by $V^\perp$ (pronounced "V perp").

The null space is the complete orthogonal complement of the row space. If a vector $v$ is orthogonal to the null space, it must be in the row space. If it weren't, we could add $v$ as an extra row to $A$ without changing its null space, which would increase the rank $r$ and break the dimension law $r + (n - r) = n$.

The Fundamental Theorem of Linear Algebra
  • $N(A)$ is the orthogonal complement of the row space $C(A^T)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Dimensions: $(n - r) + r = n$.
  • $N(A^T)$ is the orthogonal complement of the column space $C(A)$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$. Dimensions: $(m - r) + r = m$.

5. The True Action of A

Because the row space and null space are orthogonal complements, every vector $x$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ can be cleanly split into two perpendicular components: a row space component $x_r$ and a null space component $x_n$.

When the matrix $A$ multiplies $x = x_r + x_n$, we can see exactly what the matrix is doing under the hood:

  • The null space component is destroyed: $A x_n = 0$.
  • The row space component is mapped to the column space: $A x_r = Ax$.

Every vector is mapped into the column space. Furthermore, every vector $b$ in the column space comes from oneâ€”and only oneâ€”vector $x_r$ in the row space. If we ignore the null spaces, every matrix contains an invertible $r \times r$ core mapping the row space directly to the column space.

6. A Note on Bases

To effectively build these subspaces, we rely on bases. A basis consists of linearly independent vectors that span the space. Interestingly, if you know the dimension of the space, checking one of these properties automatically verifies the other:

  • Any $n$ independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ must span $\mathbb{R}^n$. They form a basis.
  • Any $n$ vectors that span $\mathbb{R}^n$ must be independent. They form a basis.

By taking the bases for the row space (dimension $r$) and the null space (dimension $n-r$), we gather exactly $n$ independent vectors. Together, they span all of $\mathbb{R}^n$. This guarantees that any vector $x$ can be fully accounted for by our $x_r + x_n$ splitting, reinforcing the elegance of orthogonal complements.
