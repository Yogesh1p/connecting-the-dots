Definitions

Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\). A non-zero vector \(\vec{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n\) is an eigenvector of \(A\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\in\mathbb{R}\) if

\[ A\vec{x}=\lambda\vec{x} \]

Equivalently, \(\vec{x}\) spans a one-dimensional subspace invariant under \(A\), i.e. \(A\vec{x}\in\text{span}(\vec{x})\).

Finding Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Proof

Starting from the eigenvalue equation \(A\vec{x}=\lambda\vec{x}\) with \(\vec{x}\neq\vec{0}\), move all terms to one side:

\[ (A-\lambda I)\vec{x}=\vec{0} \]

So \(\vec{x}\) is a nonzero solution of this homogeneous system. That is possible only when \(A-\lambda I\) is singular, which is equivalent to

\[ \det(A-\lambda I)=0 \]

Therefore the eigenvalues are exactly the values of \(\lambda\) that make \(\det(A-\lambda I)\) equal to zero.

3-Step Algorithm
  1. Compute \(\det(A-\lambda I)\). This is a polynomial in \(\lambda\) of degree \(n\) called the characteristic polynomial.
  2. Solve \(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\). Its \(n\) roots (counting multiplicity) are the eigenvalues.
  3. For each \(\lambda\), solve \((A-\lambda I)\vec{x}=\vec{0}\) to find the eigenvectors (the null space of \(A-\lambda I\)).

Key checks.

  • \(\lambda_1+\cdots+\lambda_n=\operatorname{tr}(A)\)
  • \(\lambda_1\cdots\lambda_n=\det(A)\)

Consequences of \(A\vec{x}=\lambda\vec{x}\).

\[ A^k\vec{x}=\lambda^k\vec{x},\quad A^{-1}\vec{x}=\lambda^{-1}\vec{x},\quad (A+cI)\vec{x}=(\lambda+c)\vec{x} \]

Algebraic and Geometric Multiplicity

  • Algebraic multiplicity (AM): number of times \(\lambda\) appears as a root of \(\det(A-\lambda I)\)
  • Geometric multiplicity (GM): dimension of \(\operatorname{Null}(A-\lambda I)\)
Viewpoint Measuring Name
Algebra Times \(\lambda\) is a root of \(\det(A-\lambda I)\) AM
Geometry Independent eigenvectors produced GM
\[ 1\le \mathrm{GM}(\lambda)\le \mathrm{AM}(\lambda) \]

The total number of independent eigenvectors equals \(\sum \mathrm{GM}(\lambda_i)\le n\).

Rank and eigenstructure

Rank tells you how many dimensions survive under \(A\), not which special directions survive unchanged.

  • If \(\operatorname{rank}(A)=r\), then nullity is \(n-r\), giving \(n-r\) eigenvectors with \(\lambda=0\)
  • Rank does not determine diagonalisability

Diagonalisation

Let \(A\) be \(n\times n\) with eigenvectors \(\vec{x}_1,\dots,\vec{x}_n\) placed as columns of \(X\). If \(X\) is invertible:

\[ A=X\Lambda X^{-1},\qquad \Lambda=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n) \]
Proof

The columns of \(AX=X\Lambda\) say \(A\vec{x}_k=\lambda_k\vec{x}_k\) for each \(k\). If the columns are independent, \(X\) is invertible.

Powers.

\[ A^k=X\Lambda^kX^{-1} \]
Diagonalisability criterion
\[ A \text{ is diagonalisable } \Longleftrightarrow \mathrm{GM}(\lambda)=\mathrm{AM}(\lambda) \]

In particular, distinct eigenvalues always imply diagonalisability.

Symmetric Matrices and the Spectral Theorem

Spectral Theorem
  1. All eigenvalues are real
  2. Distinct eigenspaces are orthogonal
  3. \(S=Q\Lambda Q^T\)

Positive Definite Matrices

Positive Definite

A symmetric matrix \(S\) is positive definite if

\[ \vec{x}^TS\vec{x}>0\quad\forall \vec{x}\neq 0 \]
Five Equivalent Tests for Symmetric \(S\)
  1. All eigenvalues \(>0\)
  2. All pivots \(>0\)
  3. Leading principal minors \(>0\)
  4. \(S=A^TA\)
  5. \(S=R^TR\)

Positive Semidefinite Matrices

\(S\) is positive semidefinite if \(\vec{x}^TS\vec{x}\ge 0\) for all \(\vec{x}\). Equivalently, all eigenvalues are nonnegative.

Closure under addition

If \(S\) and \(T\) are symmetric positive definite, then so is \(S+T\).

Cholesky Factorisation

Every symmetric positive definite matrix admits a unique factorisation

\[ S=R^TR \]

where \(R\) is upper triangular with positive diagonal entries.

  1. \(A_1\): rectangular, independent columns
  2. \(A_2\): upper triangular Cholesky factor
  3. \(A_3=Q\Lambda^{1/2}Q^T\): symmetric square root
