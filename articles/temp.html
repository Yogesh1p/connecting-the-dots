Chapter 2 · Representation

How Do We Represent a Word?

From integers to geometry — learning meaning from context

The Naive Approach and Why It Fails

The most obvious answer: assign each word a unique integer. Fix a vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ of size $V$ and map each word to an index:

$$\text{coffee} \mapsto 1, \quad \text{tea} \mapsto 2, \quad \text{the} \mapsto 3, \quad \dots$$

This is compact and unambiguous, but it smuggles in a false assumption: that numbers are ordered. The model would be forced to treat "tea" as between "coffee" and "the" in some meaningful sense. It is not. Any arithmetic on these integers would be nonsense.

One-Hot Vectors

Instead of a single integer, give each word a vector of length $V$ that is all zeros except for a single $1$ at the position corresponding to that word:

Interactive — One-Hot Encoding

Every pair is exactly distance $\sqrt{2}$ apart. "Coffee" is as far from "tea" as it is from "the" — the representation carries no information about relatedness.

Every pair of distinct words is exactly equidistant: $\|\mathbf{e}_w - \mathbf{e}_{w'}\| = \sqrt{2}$ for all $w \neq w'$. Every dot product between distinct words is zero. Coffee is exactly as far from tea as it is from "the." The representation contains no information about which words are related.

A model learning that "coffee" is a drink cannot apply that knowledge to "tea" — from the representation's perspective, they are unrelated objects. For a vocabulary of hundreds of thousands of words, this is a fundamental barrier.

What We Actually Need

The distributional hypothesis: words that appear in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings.

We need a representation where similar words are close together in some geometric space. The key insight comes from linguistics: context is meaning.

Demonstration — Context Reveals Meaning
I drank a hot grutt this morning.
She ordered a blarpen at the café.
The grutt was bitter and dark.
He prefers blarpen to tea.

Shared context words — found near both unknown words:

Count Vectors and Their Limits

The most direct implementation: scan a large corpus and count how often each word appears near every other word. Define a co-occurrence matrix $C \in \mathbb{R}^{V \times V}$ where $C_{ij}$ records how often word $j$ appears within a fixed window of word $i$.

Two failures of count vectors.

Scale. With $V = 100{,}000$ words, $C$ has $10^{10}$ entries. Storing and manipulating this matrix is expensive. Most entries are zero — the matrix is extremely sparse.

Rigidity. The matrix must be recomputed from scratch whenever new text is available. There is no way to update it incrementally.

The standard fix is singular value decomposition (SVD): approximate $C$ by a low-rank matrix, keeping only the $d$ most important dimensions. This compresses each row from a $V$-dimensional sparse vector into a dense $d$-dimensional vector.

Can we learn the same dense representations directly, in a single pass over text?

Learning Vectors Directly: Word2Vec

Word2Vec is a family of shallow neural network models designed to learn dense vector representations directly from raw text. Rather than assigning meaning by hand, it learns word vectors by solving a prediction task: a word should have a useful vector if that vector helps predict the words that appear around it.

Word2Vec turns representation learning into a prediction problem: a word vector is useful if it helps predict the words that appear nearby. Similarity in meaning is therefore learned through similarity in context.

Each word $w$ is assigned a trainable vector $\mathbf{v}_w \in \mathbb{R}^d$. All vectors are collected into an embedding matrix $E \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$, where column $i$ holds the vector for the $i$-th vocabulary word.

The Skipgram Objective

The training task: given a center word, predict which words appear nearby. Click any word below to see its training pairs.

For each pair, the model scores compatibility between center word $c$ and context word $o$ using their dot product $\mathbf{u}_o^\top \mathbf{v}_c$. When two vectors point in the same direction, their dot product is large. Converting scores to probabilities via softmax:

$$p(o \mid c) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{u}_o^\top \mathbf{v}_c)}{\displaystyle\sum_{w \in \mathcal{V}} \exp(\mathbf{u}_w^\top \mathbf{v}_c)}$$

What the Gradient Actually Does

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{v}_c} \log p(o \mid c) = \underbrace{\mathbf{u}_o}_{\text{observed context}} \;-\; \underbrace{\sum_{x \in \mathcal{V}} p(x \mid c)\,\mathbf{u}_x}_{\text{model's expected context}}$$
This is a correction signal. The update moves $\mathbf{v}_c$ toward the vector of the word that actually appeared and away from the weighted average of all words the model currently predicts. Words that share contexts get pulled together; words that never share contexts drift apart.

CBOW: The Flip Side

Skipgram asks: given this word, what surrounds it? Continuous Bag of Words (CBOW) flips the question: given the surroundings, what is the missing word? The context vectors are averaged and fed through the same softmax to predict the center.

What the Geometry Encodes

After training, the embedding space shows a simple but useful structure: some word relationships appear as consistent shifts (offsets) between vectors. This behavior is not explicitly programmed — it emerges from the training process.

This structure is what makes embeddings useful. They do more than store words: they capture relationships between them. If a model learns several country–capital pairs, it effectively learns the idea of a "capital city" as a vector shift reusable across different countries.

From Words to Tokens

Modern models relax the assumption of whole words, operating instead on tokens — units of text that may correspond to a full word, part of a word, or punctuation. Subword tokenization dramatically lowers the risk of out-of-vocabulary failures, because even unfamiliar words can be constructed from familiar fragments.

For example, "retriever" → ["retriev", "er"]. Even if the full word is unseen, its parts may be familiar.
In practice, we no longer construct word vectors using skip-gram, CBOW, or GloVe. We initialize $E$ randomly and let the model learn it end-to-end — gradient descent discovers the same structure implicitly. What these methods gave us is not a recipe but an understanding: dense vectors can encode meaning, and context is how meaning is learned.

Relaxing orthogonality increases capacity.

In an embedding space of dimension $n = 1000$, requiring exact orthogonality allows at most 1000 independent directions. Relaxing to near-orthogonality (angles ~85°, so $\varepsilon \approx 0.087$) yields $\varepsilon^2 n \approx 7.5$, meaning the number of approximately independent directions scales as $M \sim 2^{7.5} \approx 180\times$ the strict limit — potentially hundreds of thousands of distinguishable directions in a moderate-dimensional space.
