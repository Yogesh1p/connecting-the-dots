Let's Test This.

Don't just read the ideas — probe them. Each experiment below is designed to break an assumption and show you what's actually happening under the hood.

What these experiments are

Every section of Chapter 1 builds an intuition. These experiments are designed to stress-test those intuitions — to ask: is this actually doing what I think it is?

The experiments run in Google Colab. No setup required. The code is minimal and annotated — the goal is not to build a system but to see something clearly.

Experiments
Exp 01 Word Sense Disambiguation — does self-attention actually learn, or was it already in GloVe?

The setup is minimal by design. Two sentences, one shared ambiguous word, two possible next words:

# The two input sentences sentences = [ ["new","atm","opened","near","my","home","i","am","going","to","bank","to","get"], ["there","is","a","river","at","my","home","i","am","going","to","bank","to","get"] ] # The two candidates the model must choose between candidates = ["money", "water"]

Both sentences end at "bank to get" — the word "bank" is identical in both. The only signal distinguishing a financial bank from a riverbank is the surrounding context. A small model is trained on GloVe embeddings to predict the correct candidate: money for sentence 1, water for sentence 2. Through backprop, the embedding of "bank" itself gets updated — it is no longer the static GloVe vector.

The three columns track what changes. Column 1 is the raw GloVe dot product of the original "bank" vector against every other word in each sentence — before any training. Column 2 is the same dot product, but now using the trained "bank" vector against the original GloVe vectors of the context words — this shows how the "bank" embedding shifted under training pressure. Column 3 is where the attention scores actually land after training.

Does the raw GloVe vector of "bank" already carry enough signal to distinguish the two contexts — or does that separation only emerge once the embedding is updated by training?

Exp 02 Where in the network does the murderer get named? — tracing the residual stream across layers

A murder mystery with exactly one possible answer. John and Sarah are explicitly ruled out. Michael is explicitly placed at the scene. The story ends on the word "was" — and that is exactly where we look:

STORY = ( "Three people lived together in a small house: John, Michael, and Sarah. " "One night a terrible crime happened in the house. " "The police knew that one of the three people living there must be the murderer. " "John was visiting his parents in another city that entire day. " "Sarah was attending a conference far away and had travel records proving it. " "Michael was the only one who remained in the house that night, " "if anyone was the killer that must be him. " "After reviewing the evidence, the police concluded that the murderer was" )

At every layer, the attention and feed-forward sublayers each write a small update — \(\Delta_\text{attn}\) and \(\Delta_\text{ffn}\) — into the residual stream at the position of "was". These deltas accumulate. By the final layer, \(x^{(L)}\) at "was" has to carry enough information about the entire preceding context to predict the next token. We measure \(\cos(x^{(l)},\, e_\text{name})\) at each layer for John, Michael, and Sarah — watching how the accumulated deltas push that vector across 36 layers.

Does \(x^{(l)}\) at "was" drift toward Michael gradually as the deltas stack up — or does it stay flat and only commit in the final few layers?

Exp 03 Does the model update its suspicion as evidence arrives? — tracking "murderer" alignment across the story

The same murder story, but this time fed in seven incremental chunks — each checkpoint adds one new piece of narrative. At each checkpoint, we read the final-layer vector of the last token and measure \(\cos(x^{(L)},\, e_\text{name})\) against each candidate, where \(e_\text{name}\) is the embedding of the word "murderer".

# The 7 checkpoints [1] Intro 51 tokens # three people, one house [2] Crime 79 tokens # something terrible happened [3] John's alibi 98 tokens # John was out of town [4] Sarah's alibi 121 tokens # Sarah was at a conference [5] Physical evidence 154 tokens # evidence points inward [6] Michael seen 176 tokens # Michael alone in the house [7] Conclusion prompt 190 tokens # "the murderer was" candidates = ["John", "Michael", "Sarah"]

At each checkpoint, we ask: how closely does the final-layer representation of the last token align with the embedding of "murderer" for each of the three candidates? The story systematically eliminates John and Sarah and implicates Michael — and we watch whether those alignment scores move in step with the narrative, or only at the very end.

Does \(\cos(x^{(L)},\, e_\text{murderer})\) for Michael rise as he gets implicated — and do John and Sarah's scores fall as their alibis are established? Or does the model stay uncommitted through most of the story and only shift at checkpoint 7?

Run it and share what you find

Plot the three curves across the seven checkpoints. Does Michael's score climb steadily from checkpoint 5 onward, or does it only spike at 7? Do the alibi checkpoints (3 and 4) visibly suppress John and Sarah, or do their scores barely move? The shape of these curves tells you whether the model is reasoning incrementally through the story or just reacting to the final prompt.

Exp 04 Can the model resolve a logical constraint across layers? — tracking "knight" vs "knave" alignment at the final token

A knights-and-knaves puzzle: knights always tell the truth, knaves always lie. The puzzle ends mid-sentence — the model must complete it with the correct label for Benny:

STORY = ( "On the island of Wantuutrewan there are only knights who always tell " "the truth and knaves who always lie. " "You meet four islanders: Aiken, Benny, Candy and Dueet. " "Aiken says: Benny never tells the truth. " "Aiken says: Candy always lies. " "Dueet says: Candy is a knave and I am a knave. " "After careful logical reasoning, we can conclude that Benny is a" )

Resolving this requires chaining through the statements: Dueet claims both Candy and himself are knaves — but a knave can't truthfully call themselves a knave, so Dueet is lying, making him a knave. From Dueet being a knave we can infer things about Candy and Aiken — and from Aiken's claim about Benny, we can resolve Benny's identity. The answer is logically forced.

We read \(x^{(l)}\) at the position of the final token "a" across every layer, measuring \(\cos(x^{(l)},\, e_\text{knight})\) and \(\cos(x^{(l)},\, e_\text{knave})\) at each layer. As the deltas accumulate at that token, the vector shifts — and those shifts trace out how the logical resolution actually unfolds inside the network.

How does the alignment at the final token change layer by layer — where does the "knight" curve start, where does it cross "knave", and what does the shape of that crossing look like? Is the separation sharp and late, or does it build steadily from the middle layers?

Run it and share what you find

Plot both curves across all layers. Look for where they cross and how cleanly they separate. Does the knave curve start high and fall, or never meaningfully rise? Compare the shape to Exp 02 — a murder mystery resolved by elimination versus a puzzle resolved by logical chaining. Does the model commit earlier or later? Does it look more decisive or more gradual?
